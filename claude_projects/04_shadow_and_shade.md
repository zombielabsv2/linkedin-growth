# Shadow and Shade — The Honest Manager's Playbook

## About This Project

Management book for first-time and mid-level managers in tech/enterprise. The central tension is "Good vs Nice" -- the daily choice every manager faces between doing what feels kind and doing what builds long-term growth. This is not another cheerful management book. It's about the shadow you cast as a leader (your influence, intended or not) and the shade your team provides (comfort that can become complacency).

Target audience: First-time and mid-level managers, especially in tech/enterprise companies.
Format: Short practical book, ~40K words (think Julie Zhuo's "The Making of a Manager").
Differentiation: Medium overall, but the "Good vs Nice" framing is a genuinely fresh tension nobody else owns.
Percent written: ~40%. The seed posts below form the core. A 15-post LinkedIn series has been drafted (see Series Posts section below).

First 90 Days as a Manager:

1. Observe organizational culture - Rather than rushing to implement changes, new managers should study how work gets accomplished, how achievements are recognized, and what level of authenticity is acceptable in the workplace.

2. Create a personal "user guide" - Managers should communicate their working preferences and encourage team members to share theirs. "We are coaching, supporting and managing people - not machines," noting individual needs vary and evolve over time.

### Dealing With An Underappreciated Team Member
**Date:** January 10, 2023 | **Likes:** 134 | **Comments:** 6

Q: How should I deal with my team member who is feeling underappreciated and insecure?

A: Some thoughts:

1. Nearly everyone experiences feelings of underappreciation and insecurity at various points in their career.

2. However, this requires action. "Your team member's feelings are your business. Not *only* your business but your business nonetheless."

3. Discover their preferred appreciation method - immediate feedback, public recognition, monetary rewards, or visibility to leadership. Ask directly rather than assume.

4. Examine whether favoritism (real or perceived) might explain their insecurity, even unintentionally.

5. Consider whether their role carries equivalent importance as peers in your assessment and your supervisor's perspective.

6. Understand their broader life context. Work might represent their sole stable element during personal difficulties, justifying increased emotional needs.

7. Challenge beliefs about rationing appreciation. "Infinite amount of appreciation can be produced. By you. Don't ration appreciation!" People don't become spoiled - they gain energy for demanding work.

8. Monitor how visibly you recognize their achievements versus peers' accomplishments in emails and reports. Inconsistent visibility breeds insecurity.

Key insight: Environmental factors - not inherent predisposition - create insecurity. Managers can reshape workplace environments constructively through genuine recognition.

### Managing A New Hire From Another Org
**Date:** January 13, 2023 | **Likes:** 100 | **Comments:** 17

Q: How do I manage this person that I have hired from another org?

A: Five key points:

1. Understand their previous environment - manager style, org culture, communication preferences (oral vs. written), decision-making pace, etc. Compare and contrast their old and new contexts.

2. Avoid abrupt changes without support. "Help them deal with it at a digestible rate."

3. Articulate goals and ask their approach. Begin with trust rather than skepticism to minimize long-term management overhead.

4. Showcase concrete examples of your standards for both outcomes and methods. Eliminate guesswork about expectations.

5. Publicly recognize attributes you want replicated. Peer observation drives motivation.

Closing philosophy: "An organization's culture is who they hire, promote and fire. Nothing more, nothing less." Stated values mean nothing without aligned practices in hiring, performance management, and separation policies. Demonstrate your beliefs through actions.

### Stakeholder Management
**Date:** March 24, 2022 | **Likes:** 243 | **Comments:** 22

A colleague sought my inputs on stakeholder management today, especially when playing an advisory & enablement role. Here's what I shared with them:

1. Train and develop yourself to start thinking from the perspective of the person you are stakeholder managing: 1) What is important to them 2) What level are they operating at - their vantage point 3) What energy & vocabulary do they use to describe organizational realities & opportunities

2. Most senior stakeholders constantly, but privately, worry about whether they are connected enough to the reality on the ground. In a trusted relationship, it may be helpful to share your perspective on the "Red", "Amber" and "Green" in their world - from your perspective (call it that)

3. Mask the complexity of your world - they don't have the cognitive space to deal with their complexity *and* your complexity. Be like google dot com - the UI has remained as a single textbox for 20+ years!

4. Don't share more than 2-3 things with an Exec stakeholder - they have way too many meetings in a week to process or register a longer list from you

5. Give them the confidence that you have things under control - reduce their list of things to worry about

6. Ask open ended questions when seeking their inputs. Closed ended questions will elicit no or not very helpful inputs and it would be a lost opportunity for you.

7. Seek feedback regularly and make it easy for them to give you the feedback

None of the above is easy. No one does a perfect job of all of the above, all the time. We are all learning!

## Voice & Style Guide

Rahul's writing voice has distinct characteristics that must be preserved and amplified:

**Tone:** Warm authority. He writes as a senior leader who has done the work, not as a consultant theorizing from the sidelines. He earns the right to advise by sharing his own mistakes and growth edges first.

**Structure patterns:**
- Opens with a bold, often paradoxical statement or question ("Every single day you will have a choice to make: to be a good manager or to be a nice manager")
- Uses numbered lists heavily -- practical, scannable, actionable
- Closes with a direct question to the reader ("Which one will you be?") or a resonant one-liner
- Alternates between the Q&A format (question as hook, then numbered answer) and the reflective essay

**Language:**
- Uses Indian English naturally ("wrt", "Rs.", occasional Hindi/Urdu references)
- Mixes corporate vocabulary with philosophical/Buddhist references without it feeling forced
- Favors parallel construction and antithesis ("Good may be remembered, privately. Nice will be celebrated, publicly.")
- Uses italics for emphasis on key contrasts (*only*, *not*, *end up*)
- References specific people, places, and institutions (Google, IIM-A, Randy Pausch, Jiro Ono) rather than vague allusions

**What makes it distinctive:**
- Management advice grounded in specific stories (the Australian salesperson, the SVB collapse, the Noogler cohort)
- Frameworks always get NAMED (O-C-D, Shadow/Shade, Good/Nice)
- Buddhist philosophy woven in naturally, never preachy
- Vulnerability about his own mistakes is always in service of a lesson, never self-indulgent
- No generic LinkedIn motivation -- every insight is rooted in a lived experience from Google, enterprise consulting, or cross-cultural leadership

**What to avoid:**
- Abstract inspirational content without a specific story anchor
- Leading with the conclusion (the strongest posts create a gap the reader must close)
- Long-form article format (algorithm kills it; keep to feed-length posts)
- Teaching without asking -- always end with a question or challenge

### Post 1: Good Manager vs Nice Manager — The Daily Choice

**Shadow and Shade | Part 1**

It's 4pm on a Thursday. Your top performer has just delivered a strategy deck that's... fine. Competent. But you've seen what they're capable of, and this isn't it. They look tired. They've had a rough quarter personally.

You have two moves.

The nice move: "Looks great, let's ship it." They smile. You smile. Everyone goes home feeling good. You avoid conflict. The relationship stays warm.

The good move: "This is solid work, but I think you can push the insight on slide 7 much further. Let's talk about what's holding this back." They look deflated. The meeting runs long. You feel like a jerk walking to your car.

Here's what I've learned over two decades of managing people. The payoff of "nice" matures in weeks. The payoff of "good" matures in years and decades.

Good builds systems, processes, and people who eventually don't need you. Nice builds dependency and a reputation for being easy to work with.

Good may be remembered privately. Nice will be celebrated publicly.

Good grows in character. Nice grows in charter.

Good is centered around purposes. Nice is centered around persons.

Neither is wrong. That's the part nobody tells you. Management is not a morality play. It's a daily portfolio of choices, and the mix changes with every person, every situation, every season.

You can be good. Nice can be you. The best managers I know toggle between the two with intention, not habit.

Which one did you default to today — and was it the right call?

### Post 2: Shadow of the Leader, Shade of the Team

**Shadow and Shade | Part 2**

I walked into a team meeting last year and noticed something odd. Everyone was using the same three phrases I'd been using in my emails. The same framing. The same priorities, almost word-for-word.

At first, it felt validating. My message was landing! Then it felt unsettling. Were they thinking, or were they mirroring?

This is the **Shadow of the Leader** — the well-documented idea that leaders set the temperature. Your mood, vocabulary, priorities, and behaviors percolate through your team in ways big and small. Thermostat leadership. You set the dial, the room adjusts.

But there's a second force nobody talks about: the **Shade of the Team**.

Your team also shapes what you believe to be true. Their fears become your fears. Their anxieties about a partner org become your suspicions. Their enthusiasm for a project inflates your confidence in it. You think you're reading the business. You're often reading your team's interpretation of the business.

Thermometer leadership. You think you're setting the temperature. You're actually just reporting it.

The best leaders I've watched operate hold both in tension. They cast intentional shadow — modeling the behaviors they want replicated. And they step out of the shade periodically — verifying with their own eyes, their own conversations, their own data.

The dangerous state is when you can't tell which one is operating. When you believe your convictions are yours but they've actually been inherited from the three people you talk to most.

Are you setting the temperature in your organization — or just reading it?

### Post 3: The First 90 Days

**Shadow and Shade | Part 3**

New role. New team. Day one. You've got ideas. You've seen what worked at your last company. You know exactly what needs to change.

Stop.

I've watched smart, capable leaders destroy their credibility in the first month by fixing things that weren't broken — or by fixing the right things in the wrong order, without understanding why they existed in the first place.

Your team has been building something long before you arrived. They've made tradeoffs you don't understand yet. The thing that looks like dysfunction might be a scar from a battle they fought and won.

Four things I wish someone had told me before my first leadership transition:

**1. Observe the culture before you touch it.** How does work actually get done? How are achievements recognized? What level of candor is safe? The org chart tells you nothing. The hallway conversations tell you everything.

**2. Create a personal "user guide."** Share how you work — and ask your team to share how they work. We are managing people, not machines. Their needs vary and evolve. Eliminate guesswork early.

**3. Read *The First 90 Days* by Michael Watkins.** Not because it's gospel, but because it will force you to be intentional. Your team deserves preparation, not improvisation.

**4. Develop cross-cultural sensitivity.** If your team spans geographies, do not assume the narrative that worked with a homogeneous team will translate. Invest in understanding before you invest in changing.

The urge to prove yourself quickly is strong. Resist it. The best first impression a leader can make is curiosity, not conviction.

What did you learn (or wish you'd learned) in your first 90 days as a manager?

### Post 4: The Hardest Conversation

**Shadow and Shade | Part 4**

Nobody writes about this one. Not really.

You're sitting across from someone you hired. Someone whose family you've met at the team dinner. Someone who told you last month that this role was the best thing that happened to their career.

And you're about to end it.

I've had this conversation more times than I wish. It never gets easier. Anyone who tells you it does is either lying or shouldn't be managing people.

Here's what actually happens. You rehearse the words for days. You tell yourself it's the right decision — and it probably is. The performance data is clear. The coaching conversations haven't landed. The team is carrying weight that isn't sustainable.

And still, at 2am the night before, you're staring at the ceiling wondering: Did I do enough? Did I give them a fair shot? Am I the reason this didn't work?

What I've learned:

**Dignity is non-negotiable.** However the decision was made, the conversation must be humane. Private. Direct. No ambiguity, no corporate euphemisms. They deserve to understand what happened and why.

**Don't outsource the hardest part.** If you managed them, you deliver the news. Not HR. Not your boss. You.

**It will cost you something.** And it should. If letting someone go doesn't weigh on you, you've lost something important as a leader. The guilt is not a bug. It's a signal that you understand what you're holding — someone's livelihood, identity, and sense of self-worth.

**Afterward, resist the urge to over-justify.** To yourself or to the team. The decision is made. Treat the departing person with grace. The team is watching how you handle this more closely than any all-hands you'll ever give.

The hardest part of management isn't building a team. It's knowing when someone isn't on the right team — and having the courage to act on it with decency.

What's the hardest people conversation you've ever had to navigate?

### Post 5: The Insecure Team Member

**Shadow and Shade | Part 5**

She was one of the most talented people on my team. Technically sharp, deeply thoughtful, consistently delivered quality work.

And she was constantly worried it wasn't enough.

Every 1:1, she'd ask: "How am I doing? Am I meeting expectations?" Not as a power move. As genuine anxiety. She'd been in an environment before where feedback only came during performance reviews — and it was never good.

Here's what I've come to believe: insecure team members aren't broken. The environment made them this way. A previous manager who played favorites. A culture that only celebrated the loudest voices. A review process that felt arbitrary.

The instinct for many managers is to reassure once and move on. "You're doing great." Box checked.

That's not enough. Here's what actually works:

**Ask how they want to be recognized.** Some people want public shout-outs. Others would rather have a quiet, specific message. Some want visibility to your leadership. Don't assume — ask.

**Check your own favoritism.** Not the intentional kind — nobody thinks they play favorites. The unconscious kind. Whose work do you reference in your emails? Whose name comes up first when you talk to your boss? Inconsistent visibility breeds insecurity faster than anything else.

**Don't ration appreciation.** This is the one I had to unlearn. Infinite amounts of appreciation can be produced. By you. It costs nothing. People don't become spoiled by recognition. They get energized for the demanding work ahead.

**Understand their full context.** Work might be the only stable thing in their life right now. That's not weakness. That's trust — and it's your job to honor it.

The environment you create determines whether talent thrives or shrinks. Which one is your team producing?

### Post 6: Hiring People Better Than You

**Shadow and Shade | Part 6**

I once sat across from a candidate who was clearly smarter than me in every dimension that mattered for the role. Sharper analytical instincts. Broader industry knowledge. More creative problem-solving.

My first feeling wasn't excitement. It was discomfort.

Let me be honest about something that leadership books skip over: hiring people better than you has a real ego cost. You go from being the expert in the room to being the person who hired the expert. Your identity shifts from "the one who knows" to "the one who assembled."

And that shift is exactly the point.

Here's the math I keep coming back to. If every year you bring in one person who pushes you to improve by 20%, and you do this over a 20-year career:

(1.2)^20 = 224x compounding.

That's not just their talent. That's the knowledge you absorb, the networks you access, the inspiration that compounds. 224 times what you started with.

But the ego pulls in the opposite direction. The instinct is to hire people who make you feel smart — who ask you for guidance, who look up to your experience, who validate your position. Comfortable hires. Safe hires.

Safe hires don't compound. They reassure.

The leaders I admire most share a trait: they are genuinely, visibly proud when their team members outshine them. Not performatively proud. Actually proud. Because they understand that the job isn't to be the smartest person in the room. It's to build the room.

The compounding only works if you can tolerate being the least impressive person in a meeting you organized.

Can you?

### Post 7: Keeping the Gadfly Alive

**Shadow and Shade | Part 7**

Every team has one. That person who, when the room is buzzing with enthusiasm about the new plan, raises their hand and says: "What if this goes wrong?"

Nobody likes them. They spoil the mood. They urge caution when everyone else is ready to ship. They look unhappy when the rest of the team is celebrating.

They're the **gadfly** — and killing them off might be the most expensive mistake a leader can make.

In a pleasure-seeking, delusional-positivity culture, gadflies get sidelined. They get frustrated. They leave. And usually, this doesn't matter — the party gets wilder, the bonuses keep flowing.

Until it does matter.

Silicon Valley Bank was without a Chief Risk Officer for multiple quarters. The business teams kept piling on risk. It didn't matter while the bonuses grew. When it mattered, a 40-year-old institution vanished in 48 hours.

There's an old saying: "Good advice grates on the ears." Good advice given out of sincerity usually points to mistakes or weaknesses — and that is never easy to accept.

The gadfly isn't trying to ruin your momentum. They're trying to stress-test it. The difference between a critic and a gadfly is intent — the gadfly cares about the outcome, not about being right.

Here's the paradox: the person you most want to silence in the room is often the person you most need to hear.

Don't surround yourself with the monochrome palette of yes-men and yes-women.

Keep the gadfly alive. They might be the only person who can save you.

Who is the gadfly in your organization — and when was the last time you actually listened to them?

### Post 8: Managing Up

**Shadow and Shade | Part 8**

Early in my career, I'd walk into meetings with my VP carrying fifteen updates, seven risks, and three open questions. I'd dump everything on the table, expecting them to sort through it and appreciate my thoroughness.

They didn't. They looked overwhelmed and asked: "What's the one thing I need to know?"

I was managing down beautifully and managing up terribly.

Here's what took me years to learn:

**Mask your complexity.** Your stakeholders don't have the cognitive space to deal with their complexity *and* yours. Be like google.com — the UI has been a single textbox for 25 years. The infrastructure behind it is staggeringly complex. The interface is simple.

**Share 2-3 things, maximum.** Senior stakeholders have too many meetings in a week to process a longer list from you. If you share ten things, they remember zero. Share three, they remember two.

**Give confidence, not data.** What they really want to know is: are things under control? Reduce their list of things to worry about by one. That's your job.

**Ask open-ended questions.** Closed-ended questions get unhelpful answers. "Should we do X?" gets a yes or no. "What are you most worried about in this area?" gets insight you can actually use.

**Seek feedback — and make it easy.** Don't ask "Do you have any feedback?" Ask "What's one thing I could do differently in how I update you?"

The reframe that changed everything for me: **stakeholder management is customer centricity**, not a political chore. Your leadership is your internal customer. Understand their needs. Serve them well.

How are you managing the people who manage you?

### Post 9: The New Hire From Another World

**Shadow and Shade | Part 9**

She came from a company where every decision went through three levels of written approval. He came from a startup where you shipped first and asked permission never.

Both joined the same team. Both struggled in week one.

This is the most underestimated challenge in people management: the transition gap. A new hire doesn't just change companies. They change worlds — different pace, different manager style, different culture, different definition of "good work."

And most managers treat onboarding like a checkbox exercise. Here's your laptop, here's your buddy, good luck.

Five things that actually help:

**1. Understand their previous environment.** Ask about their old manager's style, their org's communication norms, the pace of decision-making. You're not just hiring a person — you're inheriting the shape of every environment that molded them.

**2. Don't expect instant adaptation.** Help them deal with change at a digestible rate. Abrupt cultural shifts without support don't build resilience — they build anxiety.

**3. Articulate goals, then ask for their approach.** Begin with trust rather than skepticism. This reduces long-term management overhead and tells them you respect their judgment.

**4. Show, don't tell.** Show concrete examples of your standards for both outcomes and methods. Eliminate guesswork about what "great" looks like here.

**5. Publicly recognize what you want replicated.** When they do something that embodies your team's values, name it in front of others. Peer observation drives motivation faster than any onboarding document.

An organization's culture is who they hire, promote, and fire. Nothing more, nothing less. Stated values mean nothing without aligned practices.

How are you helping your newest team member navigate their transition between worlds?

### Post 10: Sam Is Here

**Shadow and Shade | Part 10**

Disruptions will keep coming. Geopolitical shifts, technological upheaval, systemic dysfunction — in forms we can't predict and at a pace we can't control.

Through all of it, someone has to plan. Someone has to organize. Someone has to be the source of energy, direction, and rhythm that gets us from here to there.

In a world where it's easy to get triggered, lose motivation, and withdraw — the person who keeps showing up is the person we need most.

That person is Sam.

Sam doesn't give inspirational speeches. Sam shows up on Monday with a plan and on Friday with progress. Sam sends the update nobody asked for but everyone needed. Sam doesn't complain about the reorg — Sam figures out where the work needs to go and moves it there.

Sam delivers. Sam has credibility. Not the kind built on titles or presentations, but the kind built on a thousand small moments of doing what they said they'd do.

Sam can be a reliable program manager. Sam can be a reliable coach. Sam can be a reliable VP. Sam can be a reliable housekeeper. Sam can be a reliable surgeon.

Hope isn't a strategy, but hope is a decision. And Sam is the person who makes that decision every single day — for themselves and for the people around them. We start feeling that everything will be alright because Sam is there.

Being a reliable person will never go out of fashion. Not in bull markets or bear markets. Not in peacetime or wartime. Not in the age of AI or whatever comes after it.

Sam shows up. Sam delivers. Sam is here.

Are you being Sam for someone right now — and who is being Sam for you?

### Post 11: The Firing Conversation — What Happens After the Words

**Shadow and Shade | Part 11**

You've rehearsed it fifty times. In the shower. On the drive in. While pretending to read emails at 8:47am.

You know the words. HR reviewed the script. Your boss approved the decision three days ago. Everything is procedurally correct.

None of that helps when you're walking to the meeting room.

I wrote in Part 4 about the weight of letting someone go — the ceiling-staring, the guilt, the cost. But I didn't write about the part that haunts me more: the sixty seconds after you say the words.

The silence. Their face changing. The moment they understand this isn't a coaching conversation. Sometimes anger. Sometimes tears. Sometimes a calm that's worse than both because you know it hasn't hit them yet.

Here's what I've learned about that silence:

**Don't fill it.** The instinct is to keep talking — to explain, to soften, to over-justify. Resist. They need a moment to process. Give it to them. That silence is theirs, not yours to manage.

**What you say matters less than how you say it.** They won't remember your carefully crafted rationale. They'll remember whether you looked them in the eye. Whether your voice was steady or rehearsed. Whether you treated them like a human being or a problem being resolved.

**The team knows before you think they do.** They saw the calendar invite with no subject. They noticed you were quieter than usual this morning. How you treat the person walking out tells them everything about what happens if they stumble.

**Skip the corporate script.** "We've decided to go in a different direction" is a sentence designed to protect the company, not the person. Say what's true. They've earned that.

The hardest part isn't the conversation. It's walking back to your desk afterward and answering "How's it going?" with "Fine."

How do you carry the weight of these conversations — and who do you talk to when it's done?

### Post 12: Delivering Bad News Upward — The Career-Defining Skill Nobody Teaches

**Shadow and Shade | Part 12**

The launch missed by 40%. Not a rounding error — a genuine miss. Your team worked weekends for two months. The strategy was sound. The market didn't cooperate, and a few assumptions turned out to be wishful thinking.

Now you have thirty minutes with your VP on Thursday.

I've watched this moment make and break careers. Not the failure itself — failures happen to everyone. The moment that matters is what you do next.

The instinct is powerful and immediate: spin it. Find the metric that did go up. Blame the dependency that shipped late. Bury the real number on slide nine behind three caveats.

Your VP has seen this playbook a hundred times. They know what spin looks like. And every time you spin, you make a quiet withdrawal from your credibility account — one that's much harder to rebuild than a missed metric.

Here's the framework that changed how I deliver bad news:

**Lead with the number.** "We missed the target by 40%." No preamble, no context sandwich. The number first. Everything after it sounds more credible because you didn't try to hide it.

**Own your piece.** Not performative ownership — "I take full responsibility" — which everyone sees through. Specific ownership. "I underestimated the onboarding friction. Here's what I missed and why."

**Bring the learning, not just the loss.** What did the failure teach you that you couldn't have learned any other way? This is not spin. This is showing that the investment wasn't wasted even though the outcome was.

**Propose the fix before they ask.** "Here's what I'd do differently, and here's what I need from you to course-correct." You're not asking to be rescued. You're showing you've already started solving.

The leaders who grow fastest aren't the ones who never fail. They're the ones who deliver bad news so well that their leadership trusts them more after the failure than before.

When was the last time you delivered bad news upward — and did you lead with the truth or bury it?

### Post 13: The Peer You Can't Stand — Navigating Without Becoming Political

**Shadow and Shade | Part 13**

You're in a cross-functional meeting. The same peer is presenting your team's joint work as if they conceived, designed, and delivered it solo. Again.

You've raised it privately. Nothing changed. You've escalated diplomatically. Your manager said "try to work it out." The organizational incentive says collaborate. Your gut says this person is making your professional life measurably worse.

Here's the part nobody admits in leadership development programs: some peers are genuinely difficult to work with. Not because of miscommunication. Not because of different working styles you can bridge with a personality assessment. Because their incentives, behaviors, or values are fundamentally misaligned with yours.

And you still have to ship together.

What I've learned — painfully, over multiple attempts:

**Separate the person from the pattern.** You don't need to like them. You need to predict them. Once you understand their pattern — credit-seeking, conflict-avoidance, territory-guarding — you can design around it instead of being surprised by it every time.

**Document the work, not the grievance.** Paper trails that show who contributed what aren't political. They're professional. Shared documents, meeting notes, decision logs. When the work speaks, you don't have to.

**Find the one thing you genuinely need from each other.** Even the worst peer relationships have a transaction that works. Maybe they have executive access you don't. Maybe you have technical depth they need. Build the relationship around that exchange, not around friendship.

**Know when to stop investing.** Not every relationship is fixable. Some peers will never reciprocate. Protect your energy. Be professional, be reliable, and stop expecting transformation.

The trap is becoming the thing you resent. The moment you start playing their game — taking credit, politicking, building alliances against them — you've lost something more important than any project.

How do you handle the peer relationship that no amount of "radical collaboration" can fix?

### Post 14: When Your Star Wants to Leave — The Conversation That Reveals Your Leadership

**Shadow and Shade | Part 14**

Tuesday afternoon. Your best performer closes the door, sits down, and says: "I've been talking to a few companies."

Your stomach drops. You mentally calculate the projects at risk, the institutional knowledge walking out, the six months it'll take to backfill. The instinct is immediate: counter-offer, guilt-trip, remind them of the promotion cycle that's "just around the corner."

Stop. This moment is a mirror, and what you do next reveals more about your leadership than any all-hands you'll ever give.

Here's what I've learned to do instead:

**Listen first. Really listen.** Not to formulate your counter-argument. To understand. Is it money? Growth? A bad relationship with you? Burnout? The reason matters more than the retention. Sometimes the best thing you can do is help them leave well.

**Ask the uncomfortable question: "Am I the reason?"** Maybe you've been so focused on delivery that you stopped investing in their growth. Maybe the work got stale and you didn't notice. Maybe the promises you made last year haven't materialized. If you're the reason, a counter-offer is a bandage on a wound you created.

**Resist the counter-offer reflex.** Research consistently shows that counter-offered employees leave within 12-18 months anyway. You haven't solved the problem. You've rented time.

**Accept the paradox.** The better you develop people, the more attractive they become to the market. This is not a failure of your leadership. It's proof of it. The manager who never loses anyone to a better opportunity probably isn't building anyone worth poaching.

The hardest version of this conversation is when they should leave — and you know it. When the role has outgrown what you can offer, or the company can't match where they need to go. Saying "I think you should take it" costs you something real. It also earns you something that compounds forever: a relationship built on honesty instead of retention.

Who have you let go that you should have — and what did it teach you?

### Post 15: Managing Someone Better Than You — The Ego Test Nobody Prepares You For

**Shadow and Shade | Part 15**

She joined your team with fifteen years of domain expertise you'll never match. The engineers go to her for technical decisions, not you. In meetings, the room shifts when she speaks — not because of her title, but because she's usually right.

And she reports to you.

Every morning, a small voice asks: why would someone this good need me? Every meeting where she corrects your framing, the imposter syndrome gets a little louder. You start avoiding technical discussions. Or worse — you start overcompensating, asserting authority on things you don't understand, micromanaging to prove relevance.

I've been on both sides of this. Here's what I know:

**Your job is not to be smarter.** If you hired well — and you did — your job changes. You're no longer the expert. You're the multiplier. The person who makes their expertise reach further than it could alone.

**Context is your superpower.** They know the domain. You know the organization. The executive priorities shifting next quarter. The political currents that will determine which projects get funded. The narrative that will get their brilliant idea approved. That's value they can't generate alone.

**Air cover is invisible but essential.** Every time you absorb a fire drill, deflect an unreasonable request, or protect their focus time — you're doing work they'll never see but always benefit from. The best managers of brilliant people are often the least visible ones.

**Ask them how to be useful.** Not as a performative gesture. As a genuine question. "Given what you're working on, what's the most useful thing I can do for you this week?" The answer will surprise you, and it will be more valuable than anything you'd have guessed.

The ego cost is real and ongoing. You will sit in reviews where their work is celebrated and your contribution is invisible. You will watch them get recruited for roles above yours. And if you've done your job well, you'll feel proud instead of threatened — because the room you built is bigger than you are.

What has managing someone more capable than you taught you about what leadership actually is?
