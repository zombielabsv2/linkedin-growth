# Career Volume -- Book & Series

## About This Project

"Career Volume" is a narrative business book and LinkedIn series built around a single equation: **Length x Breadth x Depth**. The premise is that most career advice is one-dimensional (go deep, climb fast, specialize). In reality, enduring careers are three-dimensional -- they require longevity (Length), versatility across functions/industries/geographies (Breadth), and mastery plus executive presence (Depth). A career that maximizes only one dimension is a line or a surface, not a volume.

**Who it's for:** Mid-career professionals (5-15 years in), MBA students, and career pivoters -- anyone who senses their career has stalled on one axis and doesn't know why. This is the broadest commercial audience of all four planned books.

**What makes it different:** Frameworks over platitudes, grounded in real transitions (India to Australia, IP to financial services, leadership to IC -- all simultaneously). The author has lived the multi-variable career change that conventional wisdom says is impossible. Each chapter pairs a named framework (Gap vs Gain, The Manthan, The Wrong Race) with a specific autobiographical story, making abstract advice concrete and memorable. Think "Range" by David Epstein, but from a single practitioner's arc.

**Format:** Narrative business book, ~55K words. ~35% written via LinkedIn posts and series drafts.

### Post 1/10: The Career Volume Equation

In school, we learned that Volume = Length x Breadth x Depth.

Turns out, careers work the same way.

A friend called me last week. 17 years in one function at one company. Stellar performer. Just got passed over for a role she wanted badly. "I have so much experience," she said. I asked her: "Experience in what dimensions?"

She had Length -- years and years of it. But almost no Breadth across functions. And Depth? She'd stopped going deeper five years ago when the role became comfortable.

Her career had become a thin, long line. Not a volume.

Here's the framework I keep coming back to:

**Length** = years worked, stamina, longevity. The marathon dimension.
**Breadth** = functions touched, industries crossed, geographies navigated. The versatility dimension.
**Depth** = how far you've gone in your domain -- executive presence, thought leadership, mastery. The gravity dimension.

A 20-year career with no breadth is a line.
A career across five industries with no depth is a surface.
A deep expert who burns out in four years is a dot.

Volume requires all three.

The beautiful thing? You don't need to maximize all three simultaneously. At different stages, you expand different dimensions. Early career -- go deep. Mid-career -- go wide. Late career -- go long and share.

But you need to be intentional about which dimension you're expanding right now. Otherwise, someone else is choosing for you.

Which dimension of your career volume are you actively expanding today?

### Post 2/10: The Wrong Race

"How do I stay the youngest person in the room?"

A mentee asked me this with the intensity of someone preparing for battle. I told him something he didn't want to hear.

Don't be in that race.

I get it. In your 20s and early 30s, being the youngest VP, the youngest director, the youngest anything -- it feels like proof that you belong. I chased that too. The dopamine of being "ahead" is intoxicating.

But here's what nobody tells you about that race: the finish line keeps moving. And the impatience it breeds? That's the real career killer. Not being "behind."

Impatience makes you switch roles before the compounding kicks in. It makes you optimize for title over learning. It makes work a comparison sport -- and comparison is a team sport's worst enemy.

Instead of asking "Am I the youngest?" try asking these:

What are the 5 things I need to feel happy, successful, and fulfilled? Not the Instagram version. The real list.

Which of those can my current role actually give me? Painful truth: no single role gives you everything. Especially as you grow senior.

For what's missing -- where else can I find it? Hobbies, mentoring, communities, side projects, faith.

When your A through E are well-identified and well-addressed, the obsession with being youngest evaporates. You stop running someone else's race.

The people with the most enduring careers aren't the ones who arrived earliest. They're the ones who kept building after everyone else got tired.

What race are you actually running?

### Post 3/10: They Said You Can Change Only One

They said you can change only one of these at a time: role, industry, or country.

I changed all three. Simultaneously.

Here's the backstory. I had a computer science degree, an MBA, and years in intellectual property -- a niche that most people couldn't even spell correctly. I wanted to move to Australia, switch to financial services, and shift from leadership to an individual contributor role.

Every career advisor said: pick one. Change your country but stay in your industry. Change your function but stay in your geography. The "conventional wisdom" was clear -- changing multiple variables is a recipe for rejection.

So I did something different. I spent six months in deep introspection -- what I call the Manthan, the churning. And I distilled my entire career into one transferable truth:

"I work with senior CXO-level stakeholders, understand what they need in their business, and develop solutions that help them succeed."

That's it. That was the thread that connected IP to financial services, India to Australia, leadership to individual contribution. Not industry knowledge. Not functional expertise. The ability to understand a business leader's problem and build something useful.

That two-minute articulation, born from six months of churning, unlocked conversations that no resume could have.

The "change only one" rule exists because most people can't articulate what transfers. Not because multiple changes are impossible. The constraint isn't the market -- it's your clarity about what you actually bring to the table.

What's your transferable core? Not your title. Not your industry. The thing that works anywhere.

### Post 4/10: The Manthan

In Indian mythology, the Manthan is the great churning of the ocean -- gods and demons pulling a serpent back and forth around a mountain until treasures emerge from the chaos.

My career's most important turning point looked nothing like a promotion or a job offer. It looked like six months of doing nothing visible.

I was between roles. The temptation was to spray resumes everywhere, say yes to the first reasonable offer, stay busy. Instead, I chose to churn.

Every morning, I wrote. What do I actually enjoy doing? What am I genuinely good at versus what I've just been doing by default? What would I regret not trying? What story do I tell myself about my career that might not be true?

Most of those mornings produced garbage. Repetitive loops. Self-pity dressed as analysis. But slowly, like sediment settling in water, clarity emerged.

I realized my real skill wasn't in any specific domain. It was in sitting across from a business leader, understanding what kept them up at night, and translating that into a solution they could act on. Industry didn't matter. Function didn't matter.

That clarity -- hard-won, not downloaded from a career quiz -- became the foundation for every major move afterward.

Here's what I've noticed: the people who make the sharpest career pivots aren't the most networked or the most credentialed. They're the ones who did the Manthan. Who sat with the discomfort of not knowing, long enough for something true to surface.

Are you churning -- or just spinning?

### Post 5/10: Gap vs Gain

A colleague sat across from me last Tuesday. On paper -- thriving. Great title, good compensation, respected by peers. In person -- hollow.

"I feel like I'm never enough," she said.

I asked her one question: "Tell me five things you've accomplished in the last three years that you're genuinely proud of."

Silence. Then, slowly, reluctantly -- five remarkable things poured out. A product launch that changed a business unit. A team she built from scratch. A mentee who got promoted. Moving her parents to a better home. Running a half marathon after a knee injury.

She had never paused to count them.

This is the Gap vs Gain problem.

**Gap = Future minus Present.** Where I want to be versus where I am. The ever-expanding to-do list. The next promotion, the next milestone, the next zero in the bank account.

**Gain = Present minus Past.** Where I am versus where I started. What I've already survived, built, and become.

Most high achievers are Gap-obsessed. It's the fuel that got us out of middle-class constraints, year after year, decade after decade. But here's the trap: the Gap is a mirage. There will always be more. Always.

Single-minded pursuit of the Gap = recipe for burnout.
Accounting for the Gain = recipe for renewal.

Just like Earth needs both rotation and revolution to sustain life, you need both the Gap and the Gain to sustain yours. Determination for the future and gratitude for the past. Becoming and being.

You are the person you have to live with your entire life. That person deserves some applause.

When did you last account for your Gain?

### Post 6/10: Raising the Stakes

In 2005, I hung up on the one phone call that could have changed my career.

I was between jobs. A recruiter had called about a prestigious research firm opening their India office. Senior leadership role. This was it. The big one. I told myself: "This is now or never."

The firm's leader, Krishna, was going to call me at 7 PM. I started checking the landline's dial tone at 5 PM. Every five minutes. Is it working? What if it's broken? What if he already called and I missed it?

By 7 PM, I was a wreck. By 7:15, I was convinced it wasn't happening. When the phone finally rang at 7:30 and a warm voice said, "Hi Rahul, my name is Krishna. How are you doing?" -- I replied:

"I am good. Thanks. Bye."

And hung up.

I wish I was joking.

The only explanation I have, twenty years later, is that I had raised the stakes so high that my nervous system couldn't handle the actual moment. I had turned a conversation into a verdict on my entire life. And my body chose escape.

This is what self-sabotage looks like. Not dramatic acts of destruction. Just quiet moments where the pressure we manufacture exceeds our capacity to perform.

It's never now or never. Tomorrow is another day. The sun will rise again.

The next time you catch yourself treating an opportunity like your last chance at oxygen -- pause. Breathe. Lower the stakes. You'll show up better.

What stakes have you unnecessarily raised for yourself?

### Post 7/10: Speaking Someone Else's Language

Imagine you're in an elevator with Anand Mahindra. You have 60 seconds. You start talking about your "cross-functional transformation initiatives" and "platform engineering expertise."

His eyes glaze over in three seconds.

Now imagine you say: "I help businesses get to market 40% faster and save Rs. 200 crores annually in operational costs."

Different conversation entirely.

I learned this the hard way. For years, I described my work in the vocabulary of my industry. Cloud migrations. Digital transformation. Agile methodologies. When I spoke to people in tech, they nodded. When I spoke to people outside tech, I got polite smiles and zero callbacks.

The breakthrough came when I realized: every business leader, whether they run an auto company or a hospital chain, cares about exactly four things.

1. Growing revenue
2. Reducing cost
3. Moving faster
4. Keeping stakeholders happy

That's it. Everything else is implementation detail.

The moment I started translating my work into those four languages, doors opened that I didn't even know existed. Not because my skills changed -- but because my audience could finally hear me.

This applies beyond job interviews. Pitching an internal project? Don't talk about the technology. Talk about which of the four it serves. Negotiating a raise? Don't list your activities. Quantify your impact in terms the CFO cares about.

Drop your industry's jargon. Speak the language of the person across the table.

The best communicators aren't the most articulate. They're the most translatable.

Whose language do you need to learn to speak?

### Post 8/10: Choosing Environments

In raising our son, my wife and I kept asking: "Is our child fit for this school?"

The question that changed everything was the reverse: "Is this school fit for our child?"

That one reframe -- from fitting yourself into environments to finding environments that fit you -- applies to careers more than we admit.

I've watched talented people wither in prestigious companies. Not because they lacked skill but because the environment bred anxiety, not acceleration. The hours, the politics, the passive-aggressive emails, the culture of performing busyness -- it was slowly poisoning them. And they stayed because the logo on their LinkedIn profile looked good.

I've also watched average performers become extraordinary when they found the right soil. A manager who actually coached. A team that valued contribution over optics. A pace that matched their working rhythm.

The difference between burnout and brilliance is often not about the person. It's about the match.

An environment that breeds anxiety is no good for you -- regardless of the brand name. An environment that breeds growth will make you shine -- even if nobody's heard of the company.

Here's a practical test before you take your next role. Don't just ask: "Can I do this job?" Also ask:

- How does my future manager handle disagreement?
- What happens to people who fail here?
- Do people leave energized or drained at the end of the day?
- Is the culture stated or actually practiced?

An expectation mismatch will make you burn.
An exceptional match will make you shine.

Shine, don't burn.

Are you in an environment fit for you -- or are you fitting yourself into an environment?

### Post 9/10: Winter Is the Time to Build

No career goes "up and to the right" forever. Mine certainly didn't.

There were seasons where everything clicked -- promotions, recognition, momentum. And there were winters. Painful, quiet stretches where progress felt invisible and self-doubt became a daily visitor.

Here's what nobody puts on LinkedIn: the winters are where the real building happens.

We love attributing the good seasons to our hard work and the bad ones to external forces. But agency means owning both. And the most valuable thing you can do in a career winter is stop trying to force spring -- and start building instead.

**Build faith.** We seek when we suffer. If suffering leads to seeking, then suffering isn't the enemy. Netflix and Instagram will always be there. Let the winter be a time for something deeper.

**Build health.** That exercise routine you sacrificed for late-night emails? That sleep you traded for "giving your best"? Your body kept score. Let this be the time to settle the debt.

**Build relationships.** Thousands of LinkedIn connections. Hundreds of B-school contacts. When did you last have a real conversation with any of them? Dormant relationships are like dormant seeds. Water them.

**Build skills.** When the career is cruising, skill-building takes a back seat. No function and no industry is staying static. Let the winter be your classroom.

**Build a venture.** A side project. A creative outlet. Something that could become an occupation someday -- or simply keep your mind alive.

Winter is not a detour from your career. It's the foundation for the next chapter.

What are you building in your winter?

### Post 10/10: The 10-Year Question Reframed

"Where do you see yourself in 10 years?"

I've been asked this question dozens of times. I've asked it to hundreds of people I've mentored. And after 25+ years of watching careers unfold, I'm convinced it's the wrong question.

The world changes too fast for 10-year predictions. Technology disrupts industries overnight. Pandemics rearrange economies. A single conversation can redirect your entire trajectory. Pretending we can plot coordinates on a decade-long map is comforting but dishonest.

Here's what I ask instead:

**Who do you admire who is 10 years ahead of you?**

Not a celebrity. Someone you've actually observed. Maybe a senior colleague whose life seems balanced. A founder who builds with integrity. A professor who lights up when teaching. A friend's parent who retired with peace, not regret.

Now ask: what specifically about their life do I admire?

Is it their wealth? Their influence? Their relationships? Their calm? Their health? Their sense of purpose? Their gratitude?

That list -- honest, specific, personal -- is your real answer to the 10-year question. Not a job title. Not a company name. A way of being.

I did this exercise years ago. My list had nothing about becoming VP of anything. It had words like: "peace," "impact beyond my company," "time for the people I love," "body that works," "a mind that's still curious."

That list became my compass. Not a GPS with turn-by-turn directions. A compass. North doesn't change even when the terrain does.

Find your person. Study their life. Build your list. That's your 10-year answer.

Who do you admire -- and what does their life tell you about yours?

Five pathways for career transitions:

1. Same industry, same function, different company - Requires recruiter outreach, networking, or serendipitous encounters.

Career volume expands through increased length (years worked), depth (executive or expert status), or breadth (multi-functional experience).

### Youngest Person in the Room (Ch 2 seed)
**Date:** June 23, 2022 | **Likes:** 68 | **Comments:** 4

A recent conversation: How do I stay to be the youngest person in the room? My suggestions:

1. Don't be in that race. This pursuit may be a good motivator in our younger years / junior levels but it actually becomes a liability to harbor that ambition, especially by way of fostering unmanageable impatience. Impatience will lead to destruction! Being in a competition with others, which 'be the youngest' essentially is, is a disaster waiting to happen in a team sport called life (and that includes working at a company)!

2. Instead, try to answer these 3 questions:

i) What are the A, B, C, D, E that you need to feel happy, successful and fulfilled? This could be A=money, B=social equity, C=inspiration or belonging or camaraderie, D=sense of positive impact on others or society, E=innovation in any form, etc. This identification is not as easy as I might have made it sound! Spend the effort in finding your authentic responses.

ii) What among your A, B, C, D or E can your role at your workplace give you? Painful reminder: It is *not* your company's or role's responsibility to give you all you need in your life! You will be lucky if you get *all* of these at work and it is more possible at more junior levels or as a new joinee.

iii) Amongst your A, B, C, D, E - what you don't get from your company or role, where can you get it from? This could be hobby groups, volunteer organizations, faith organizations, mentoring or supporting other companies or individuals, adopting a pet, spending time with friends and loved ones, side hustles etc. When your A, B, C, D and E are all well identified and well addressed - you will not feel the need to be in the race of being the youngest in the room!

3. Try to identify the skills needed to be successful at 2 or 3 levels above your current role. Regularly assess how you are on those skills, see which ones you are motivated to become better at, dedicate yourself to building those skills and enjoy the process of improving yourself. Superior skills may give you the acceleration that you might relish!

Don't be in the race to be the youngest person in the room. Be in the race to become the best version of yourself regularly.

### Gap vs Gain (Ch 5 seed)
**Date:** April 13, 2022 | **Likes:** 166 | **Comments:** 11

Gap vs Gain

A conversation with a colleague today highlighted, once again, the need to account for the Gain and not only to agonise over the Gap.

Gain = Present - Past; how far have I come compared to where I was. What I have achieved already. In any form (money, stature, learning, experiences, relationships, satisfaction)

Gap = Future - Present; where do I want to go compared to where I am. What all I need to achieve - more. The short and the long Life Task List.

Most of us are naturally wired to keep calculating the Gap constantly and keep making plans to cover the Gap. This mindset has, obviously, worked well for us so far as a source of motivation and made us improve the circumstances we found ourselves in - year after year, decade after decade. The fuel to traverse the great wide "Middle Classness". Lower to Upper, Higher and Higher. With no end in sight!

The issue, however, with the single minded pursuit of the Gap is that it is chasing a mirage. There will *always* be a Gap. One can never become the absolute, permanent best on *all* dimensions and parameters. There's always more to do.

Single minded pursuit of the Gap = Recipe for burnout and disillusionment. It will never be enough.

Balancing that with the reflection on the Gain is likely to nourish the soul with some applause for self on a difficult path well navigated, so far. You survived so much that life threw at you. On many days, you also thrived!

We are the person we have to live with throughout our lives! Applaud that person by accounting for the Gain, regularly. This person worked to the best of their abilities given their circumstances. They deserve the applause.

Accounting for Gain = Recipe for nourishment and renewal.

Being vs Becoming. Comfort with who I am vs presumed fleeting satisfaction with who I want to become in the future.

Gratitude for the past vs Determination for the future.

Just like both Rotation and Revolution are needed to sustain life on Earth, so is true for Gap and Gain - both are needed to sustain life on Self.

Are you accounting for the Gain - pausing to applaud yourself and thanking those who helped in the journey so far?

### Doing a Global Role from India (Ch 9 seed)
**Date:** April 2, 2022 | **Likes:** 362 | **Comments:** 17

Doing a global role sitting out of India can be quite challenging, but for those of us who are keen to be in India, here are some thoughts that might work:

1. **Lead with world class expertise:** Create and promote a culture that puts domain expertise and solution development & delivery expertise over everything else. Expertise can't be ignored for long.

2. **Execution excellence:** Obsess over your deliverables, major and minor deliverables - again, setting and upholding a world class standard is an unfailing recipe and consistency in execution excellence is bound to win support among the global stakeholders.

3. **Higher bar:** This is a fact. Hard as it may be. When workflows or responsibilities move from HQ or higher cost locations to outside HQ or lower cost locations, the expectation goes *up*. Hold yourself and others around you to a high bar, even higher than what it previously was. That is the only way to build a sustainable brand for the country.

4. **Communicate regularly, clearly and consistently:** "Distance breeds fear, anxiety and doubt." Communicate weekly, monthly and quarterly. Communicate tactical updates and your strategic vision. When a choice must be made between more and less - choose to over communicate.

5. **Visit your global stakeholders often:** Even more importantly, where possible, have them visit. The power of a week long, holistic immersive experience is immense and you have their attention the whole time as opposed to visiting them. Both are needed.

6. **Hire for the global corporation and hire well:** Never dilute, instead raise, the hiring standards. Never hire only for your role or only for your team. Hire for the global corporation and hire for the industry - attract great people to your industry and give them rich roles.

7. **Be an exporter of talent:** Every hire you make for the company (and even the industry) that goes and works in other parts of the organisation creates a brand for your team and location. A gift that keeps giving. Accept the need for an always on hiring pipeline but help the organisation and *also* the individuals by exporting top talent.

8. **Achieve an optimal global location mix:** Just because you are in a certain location certainly should not mean that everyone in your organization needs to be in the same location / time zone. Solve for business needs - hire globally, align to stakeholder locations, talent availability and hire a diverse team where every new individual enriches the team, enriches the org and enriches the culture. Great people are found everywhere!

What other strategies have worked for you? What must be avoided?

Expectation mismatch to exceptional match

An environment that breeds anxiety is no good for you.

The next time you are looking for a role at a place: Don't only ask if I am fit for this place but also if this place is a fit for me.

Careers, happiness and a general sense of well being will truly thrive in a place that is fit for you.

An environment that breeds these will help you accelerate.

An expectation mismatch will cause burn out.
An exceptional match will make you shine brightly.

Shine, don't burn.

Go from anxious to accelerating.

### Quiet Quitting (Ch 11 seed)

Learned Helplessness: Quiet Quitting

I began my career at a blue-chip IT services firm as a software engineer. I felt underutilized in maintenance work and left after 18 months. A decade later, with an MBA in intellectual property, I returned to the same company in sales -- only to find it transformed.

Decisions taking ages. Excessive risk aversion. Bloated email chains and meetings. More managers than doers. Customers deprioritized.

"There was no ambition left." The middle management became bloated. Decisions and culture suffered.

This contrasts with a classical Hindi couplet about wasting life -- arguing that human existence is precious and shouldn't be squandered. Meaningful engagement matters more than "quiet quitting" -- staying employed while disengaging.

Quit Quitting not Quiet Quitting.

### Winter is the Time to Build (Ch 12 seed)
**Date:** September 3, 2022 | **Likes:** 160 | **Comments:** 8

Winter is the time to build. No company and no career can be unidirectionally 'up and to the right'. Company trajectories and career trajectories are sometimes discouragingly marred by painful periods of slump, and sometimes greatly supported by periods of good luck.

We tend to attribute the periods of good luck to our own hard work, and periods of slump as something imposed on us by something outside!

Agency is having a sense of control over life outcomes. Easy to say, difficult to practice. But, practice we must.

Periods of slump can feel like the label says. Slump.

A lot of folks, including me, have experienced periods of slump in our careers. And no one is immune to it.

My learning and, thus my, recommendation for dealing with a period of slump is to focus on building.

1. **Building faith:** We seek when we suffer. If suffering leads to seeking, then suffering isn't a bad thing. This isn't obvious and is very difficult to follow while the going is good. With Netflix, Amazon Prime, Tik Tok, YouTube Shorts and so on - who has the time to seek? Let this be that time.

2. **Building physical and mental health:** Burnout is real. Our body and mind needs time, space and effort to heal and recover. Food is medicine but we might have been eating just to get by. Sleep might have been sacrificed often so we can 'give our best'. There was never enough time to sleep 8 hours. Let this be that time.

3. **Building relationships:** We might have been accumulating thousands of LinkedIn contacts. Hundreds of folks from B School. Tens of friends from Engineering. Deepest BFF relationships from school. But, we never had the time to connect, reconnect and nourish those relationships. Let this be that time.

4. **Building skills:** When the career is going well in a stable company and a stable role, skill building takes a back seat. No function and no industry is staying static. We never have the time to fully catch up on the latest in our field of work or to contribute meaningfully and never have the time to really build skills that will help for the rest of our career. Let this be that time.

5. **Building a venture:** Irrespective of whether one was 'working 18 hours' (!) or not, there likely wasn't enough time, motivation or mental bandwidth to really think about building a side gig. Commercial or otherwise. Something that could become a real occupation down the line. We are not living in times when the start-to-finish of our careers will be one company or even one industry. Gig economy is here. Specialization is rewarded well. Never had the time so far to be a specialist outside the core field of work? Let this be that time.

Winter is coming. Or, Winter may be here already.

Faith, Health, Relationships, Skills or Venture. What will you build? Something else?

Effort vs Impact

Effort: 1-0=1, 2-1=1, 3-2=1, 4-3=1, 5-4=1...

Impact: [1-0]/0=infinite, [2-1]/1=100%, [3-2]/2=50%, [4-3]/3=33%, [5-4]/4=25%

As time progresses, let's try to increase our effort on things that are truly important to us: health, relationships, faith, learning, kindness, service, innovation - for, as time passes, the effectiveness of the same effort goes down - because expectations go up and the low hanging fruit is gone! What is worth doing is worth doing well - always!

### Mistakes I Wish I Had Not Made -- Part 1 (Ch 14 seed)
**Date:** October 14, 2022 | **Likes:** 240 | **Comments:** 14

Mistakes I wish I had not made (Part 1)

**1. Insufficient Optimism & Negativity Bias**
Our evolutionary survival instinct makes us alert to threats, yet this hypervigilance can drain others' energy. While risk management skills are valuable, pairing them with optimistic framing and enthusiasm proves more effective than pessimistic communication.

**2. Over-Seriousness About One's Role**
For those without privileged upbringings, sincere dedication enabled progress. However, excessive seriousness inhibits creativity and forward momentum. "People raised in love see the world differently than people raised in survival," suggesting context shapes professional perspective.

**3. Applying Proven Methods Universally**
Early-career success comes from solving defined problems using reliable playbooks. However, leadership demands distinguishing between problems (solvable) and polarities (manageable). "Polarities are to be managed. Problems are to be solved," cautioning against rigidly reapplying past strategies.

Key Lessons: Remain optimistic, contribute positive energy, stay open to learning.

### Mistakes I Wish I Had Not Made -- Part 2 (Ch 14 seed)
**Date:** December 24, 2022 | **Likes:** 155 | **Comments:** 10

Mistakes I wish I had not made (Part 2)

**Mistake 4: Being "Always On"**

Many professionals adopt a "work hardest" narrative to compensate for lacking exceptional intelligence. This mindset evolves into constant availability across weekends, holidays, and leave periods. This approach:

- Sets poor precedent for colleagues with different circumstances
- Damages team culture
- Harms physical and mental wellbeing
- Creates problems later when energy reserves deplete

"No one has an infinite, permanent source of energy." The lesson: careers require sustainability; burnout undermines long-term success.

**Mistake 5: Rapid Role Changes**

Early career job-hopping driven by impatience, boredom, or FOMO damages professional development. Building expertise, industry knowledge, relationships, and sponsorship requires time. Frequent changes signal instability to the market and prevent capitalizing on "slowly accruing" benefits of sustained roles.

His learning: develop deep expertise, allow decisions time to yield holistic rewards, and intentionally manage your professional signal.

### Perceptions Matter (adjacent seed)
**Date:** April 11, 2024 | **Likes:** 195 | **Comments:** 13

Perceptions matter. I went to work in Australia's financial services sector in 2012. A colleague's approach to appearance and professional presentation stood out - this person consistently wore "starched shirts, silk ties, expensive cuff links, fancy patent leather shoes, top brand suits."

When asked why such attire mattered for sales roles, the colleague explained the psychology: "When I show up dressed this well, my customers perceive me to be a very successful salesperson...That they will be left behind and be beaten by the competition if they, too, don't transform themselves with my help."

"Substance needs time to achieve the desired impact" while perception creates immediate influence. This principle applies across multiple professions - consulting deliverables, medical credentials, legal offices, and e-commerce website performance.

Before substance, perceptions matter.

## Voice & Style Guide

### Tone
- Conversational authority. Speaks as a peer who has lived it, not a guru dispensing wisdom from above.
- Warm but direct. No hedging, no corporate-speak, no "I think maybe perhaps."
- Vulnerable where it counts -- the Krishna phone call, the self-sabotage, the mistakes. Strength through honesty, not through polish.

### Structure (LinkedIn posts)
- **Hook:** Open with a scene, a question, or a counterintuitive claim. First 2 lines must stop the scroll.
- **Story:** One specific, named anecdote (a colleague, a mentee, a personal failure). Never abstract.
- **Framework:** Name it. Gap vs Gain. The Manthan. The Volume Equation. Named frameworks are memorable and shareable.
- **Punchline:** A one-line reframe or reversal near the end. "Don't be a martyr, be a hero." "Shine, don't burn."
- **Question:** Close with a question that invites reflection, not just engagement bait.

### Structure (Book chapters)
- Same pattern as posts but expanded: deeper story (2-3 pages), the framework with examples (3-5 pages), counterarguments and nuance (1-2 pages), practical exercises (1 page).
- Each chapter should stand alone as a self-contained essay while building on the L x B x D throughline.

### Language patterns
- Uses Indian cultural references naturally (Manthan, Hindi couplets, Jiro, Kalpana) without over-explaining for Western audiences.
- Mathematical metaphors (Volume equation, Effort vs Impact ratios, Rotation and Revolution).
- Contrasting pairs: Gap vs Gain, Good vs Nice, Shadow vs Shade, Burn vs Shine, Churning vs Spinning.
- Bold formatting for framework components. Numbered lists for practical advice.
- ~250-280 words per LinkedIn post. Tight. No filler.

### What to avoid
- Corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "alignment").
- Generic career advice ("follow your passion," "network more").
- Humble-bragging. The Google Director title is context, never the point.
- Lecturing tone. Every post should feel like a conversation over chai, not a keynote.
