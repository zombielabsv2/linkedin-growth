# Awakening, Assets & Access — Lessons from an Unlikely Life

## About This Project

Personal/memoir book tracing Rahul Jindal's unlikely life arc: North Indian middle class upbringing, Computer Science degree (built "the world's first Hindi speaking computer" in 2002), pivot through Intellectual Property law, pivot through Sales, pivot through Strategy consulting, relocation to Australia (where a freak accident forced him to rebuild everything), and ascent to Google Director leading AI transformation at scale. Along the way: raising a nonverbal son with autism, practicing Nichiren Buddhism, building Kari by Kriti (a D2C artisanal home decor brand), and angel investing in 71 companies.

This is not a career advice book. It's a book about what life teaches you when you stay awake to it. The framework is Awakening (purpose), Assets (skills, capital, health, time, brand), and Access (networks, relationships, visibility). The vulnerability is the differentiator -- the stories that LinkedIn posts only hint at need to be told fully.

Target audience: Broad -- professionals, aspiring leaders, Indian diaspora, anyone navigating an unconventional path.
Format: Memoir-meets-philosophy, ~60K words (think "Shoe Dog" meets Naval Ravikant).
Differentiation: Very High -- unique life arc, requires deep vulnerability.
Percent written: ~35%. The seed posts below form the skeleton. A 15-post LinkedIn series has been drafted (see Series Posts section below).

### Person, Purpose and Path
**Date:** June 8, 2022 | **Likes:** 52 | **Comments:** 9

Person, Purpose and Path

A conversation drew a parallel of the trio of Person, Purpose and Path to Rock, Paper and Scissors :)

What is most important? The Person, the Purpose the Person pursues or the Path followed by the Person towards the Purpose?

There can be many Paths towards the same Purpose - some Paths have the approval of others, some may not. There can be many Purposes a Person may pursue - some more in the service of Self and some more in the service of Others. However, if there's no Person - who will pursue any Purpose following any Path?

Thus, I think the Person is most important. The longevity of the Person is most important. If the Person gets killed (or demotivated / discouraged) then the Purpose and the Path are orphaned.

You may be pursuing a noble Purpose - transforming an organisation, a school, a church, a society or a discipline; despite all the headwinds or hostilities.

You may be driving the supremacy of an organization or a product as a fund raise professional or a salesperson. You may be convincing people about a religion or a philosophy as a preacher.

You may try many Paths - leading, influencing, building alliances, sponsoring, legitimising, coaxing, threatening, blackmailing, manipulating or a combination of these.

However, "if you - the Person - are not there in the arena / the field any more, the nobility or the potential of the Purpose to bring about a needed change also ceases."

If the subject of your advances - the Person - isn't feeling the freedom - is the Purpose or Path worth it?

Safeguard yourself! Don't get killed!

Safeguard the subject! Don't kill!

Strategize and change the Paths when needed. Yes, the Purpose is important. The Person or the Protagonist / Subject is the most important.

Don't be a Martyr, be a Hero! Live to see another day! Survival is not a shameful outcome on the Path to the attainment of the Purpose.

Don't be a Mercenary, be a Missionary! Assassination is not a glorious outcome on the Path of reform.

### Action Is A Great Distraction
**Date:** January 27, 2023 | **Likes:** 78 | **Comments:** 5

Humanity got the unprecedented experiences alright - though not the kinds anyone imagined. 2020-present: doesn't sound much fun, does it? Yes, everyone copes differently to stressful situations. Action can be a great distraction too. As The Flash says, 'Life is locomotion. If you're not moving, you're not living'.

## Voice & Style Guide

Rahul's writing voice has distinct characteristics that must be preserved and amplified:

**Tone:** Warm, philosophical, grounded in lived experience. He writes from a place of genuine reflection, not performance. The strongest posts feel like he's sharing something he's still processing, not lecturing from a podium. Vulnerability is his superpower in this book -- the posts that resonate most (Uber Drivers at 428, Bystander to Ally at 300) are the ones where he lets readers into specific, sometimes uncomfortable, moments.

**Structure patterns:**
- Opens with a question, a proverb, or a short provocative line ("What are you missing in your life?", "We can only give what we have")
- Uses specific stories as the entry point, then extracts the universal lesson
- Lists and frameworks emerge organically from narrative, not imposed from the top
- Closes with a reflective question or a call to action that invites the reader in

**Language:**
- Naturally multilingual -- Hindi proverbs ("Ishq aur mushk chhupane se nahin chhupta"), Urdu poetry (Allama Iqbal, Mirza Ghalib), Sanskrit concepts (Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam), Punjabi references
- Buddhist philosophy is woven in as lived practice, not academic reference -- he speaks of Buddhahood, awakening, and compassion as daily choices
- Indian cultural references are never explained away or apologized for -- they're just part of the texture
- Mixes the deeply personal with the professional without signaling the shift

**What makes it distinctive:**
- The "every person is a teacher" lens -- Uber drivers, schoolteachers, housekeepers, Kalpana Chawla all get the same respectful attention as Google VPs
- Multiple identity layers (North Indian, Buddhist, Google Director, autism parent, angel investor, D2C operator) create an unusual vantage point that no one else has
- Personal philosophy is tested against real life, not derived from reading lists
- Emotional range -- from the grief of the Columbia disaster to the humor of Indian Matchmaking
- The recurring theme: you are not your circumstances, you are your choices

**What to avoid:**
- Generic inspiration that could come from anyone ("Love will keep us alive" at 95 likes vs "Lessons from Uber Drivers" at 428 -- the difference is specificity)
- Abstract frameworks without story anchors (Person/Purpose/Path at 52 likes teaches but doesn't move)
- Sanitized memoir -- this book needs the full story (the accident, the son, the failures) not just the highlights
- Preachy tone -- the best posts invite rather than instruct

### Post 1: The Framework for Impact

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 1**

A few years ago, over chai that had gone cold twice, my friend Uday Arya asked me something that has never left me.

"Rahul, what are you missing?"

Not in the superficial sense. In the deep, keep-you-up-at-3-AM sense. What's the gap between where you are and the impact you know you're capable of?

That conversation became a framework I've carried ever since.

We need three things to create meaningful impact: **Awakening, Assets, and Access.**

**Awakening** is the hardest. It's the realization that your time is finite, your potential is infinite, and the distance between those two truths is where your life's work lives. Most people sleepwalk through decades. Not because they're lazy -- because no one shook them. No crisis forced the question. No conversation over cold chai cracked the shell.

**Assets** are what you bring to your purpose. Skills. Capital. Health. Time consciously allocated. Your personal brand. Not just what's in your bank account -- what's in your arsenal. You can be wide awake and still have nothing to build with.

**Access** is the network, the visibility, the relationships that surface the right opportunities. You can be awakened and armed -- but if no one knows you exist, you're a lighthouse in a desert.

Here's the uncomfortable math:

Missing Awakening + Assets + Access = **Sub-optimality.** You're effective but directionless.
Awakening + Missing Assets + Access = **Frustration.** You see the vision but can't execute.
Awakening + Assets + Missing Access = **Irrelevance.** You're ready but invisible.

Awakening + Assets + Access = **Impact.**

I've lived in each of those broken equations. Early career -- no awakening. Mid-career -- awakening but insufficient assets. Post-accident -- all three briefly stripped away, rebuilt one at a time.

Which of the three are you missing right now? That answer is your most important next step.

### Post 2: The Five Uber Drivers

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 2**

Five Uber rides in one week in the Bay Area. Five strangers. Five lessons I didn't expect.

The first driver had music blaring so loud I almost asked him to turn it down. Then I noticed his jaw clenched, eyes red. At a stoplight, he said quietly: "Going through a separation. Music helps." I sat back and let the bass fill the silence. Sometimes the loudest people are just trying to drown something out. Bear the loud music.

The second was a man in his mid-50s, clearly overqualified for airport pickups. He'd done limo work for executives, preferred the structure and the conversation. But the gig economy doesn't care about your preferences. Not everyone is operating in their ideal setup. Don't judge the role -- you don't know the story behind it.

The third barely spoke. Limited English. But the car was immaculate, the route was perfect, and he had a 4.98-star rating across thousands of rides. Words are overrated. Five-star service can be delivered in silence. Excellence speaks for itself.

The fourth was Sri Lankan. Every red light, he checked his phone. Not scrolling -- checking on his family. The economic crisis back home had made kidnappings common. His body was in San Francisco. His heart was in Colombo. Everyone carries a world you can't see. Don't mistake their distraction for disrespect.

The fifth was Eritrean. Within minutes, he was quizzing me about Amitabh Bachchan's 1980s films, Indian politics, and Australian cricket. He'd never been to India. He was just relentlessly, beautifully curious. Being curious costs close to nothing. It earns you everything.

Five drivers. Not one of them would make the LinkedIn "thought leader" list. Every single one of them taught me something real.

Everyone is inspirational -- we just need to be more awakened to get inspired from all of them.

Who taught you something unexpected this week?

### Post 3: The Freak Accident

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 3**

There's a line I wrote once that people keep asking me about:

"I learnt from a freak accident in Australia that you can always build it all over again."

I've never told the full story. Maybe because for years, I wasn't sure I had the words for it.

What I can tell you is this. There was a before and an after. In the before, I had built a life -- career momentum, stability, the things you accumulate when you do everything "right" for long enough. In the after, a significant part of that was gone. Not gradually. Not with warning. In a way that felt like someone had picked up the table and flipped it.

The first few weeks were disorientation. Not sadness exactly -- more like standing in a room where all the furniture has been rearranged in the dark. You keep reaching for things that aren't where they used to be.

Then something shifted. I remember the exact moment. I was sitting on a bench in Melbourne, watching people walk by who had no idea that my world had just cracked open. And a thought arrived, uninvited but certain:

Everything that was lost was something I had built once before. Which means I knew how to build it. The blueprints were inside me, not in the things.

Material things can be replaced. Careers can be rebuilt. What cannot be replaced is the will to start over. That's the one asset no accident, no crisis, no catastrophe can take from you -- unless you surrender it.

My Buddhist practice taught me a phrase that became my anchor in those months: "From this moment forward." Not from the moment before the accident. Not from the life I used to have. From this moment. Right here. What will I make of what remains?

What remains is always more than you think.

Have you ever had to rebuild something you thought was permanent?

### Post 4: The Son Who Changed Everything

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 4**

My son doesn't speak. Not in words you and I would recognize as language.

He is nonverbal. He has autism. And he has been my greatest teacher.

When he was young, my wife and I spent years asking the wrong question: "Is our child fit for this environment?" We measured him against milestones designed for a world that wasn't designed for him. Every missed marker felt like a verdict.

The question that changed everything was the reverse: "Is this environment fit for our child?"

That single reframe broke open something much larger than parenting. It rewired how I see diversity, potential, and what "communication" actually means.

My son communicates. Constantly. Through his eyes, his gestures, the way he reaches for your hand when he trusts you, the way he pulls away when he doesn't. If you're only listening for words, you'll miss everything he's saying. And that's on you, not him.

Here is what he taught me that no DEI training ever could:

Nature is truly diverse. Not diverse in a sanitized, corporate-poster way. Diverse at the level of thought itself. The way his mind processes the world is fundamentally different from mine -- not less, not broken, different. And diversity at the thought level is what all other diversities try to approximately achieve.

He taught me that potential doesn't announce itself on your schedule. It reveals itself when the environment is patient enough to let it.

He taught me that unconditional respect isn't a philosophy -- it's a practice. You either respect someone before they prove themselves to you, or you don't actually believe in human dignity. There is no middle ground.

My journey from bystander to ally -- for women, for underrepresented communities, for anyone the system wasn't built for -- started in my own living room. Watching a small boy navigate a world that couldn't hear him and choosing to become his voice.

Who in your world is communicating in ways you haven't learned to hear yet?

### Post 5: Kalpana Creates Reality

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 5**

On August 23, 2023, like billions of people, I watched Chandrayaan 3 touch down near the south pole of the moon. The first country to do so.

But what caught my eye wasn't the spacecraft. It was a name on the mission team: **Kalpana K**, Deputy Project Director.

Kalpana. The Hindi word for imagination.

Another Kalpana -- Kalpana Chawla -- had been the first woman astronaut of Indian origin. An alumni of my undergraduate school. She'd traveled 6.5 million miles in space, made 252 orbits of the earth. And then, on February 1, 2003, we watched a bright streak of light across the Texan sky. The Space Shuttle Columbia. She attained eternity, literally among the stars.

I remember something she once said: "After partition, my family was left without possessions but we learnt you couldn't lose by working hard."

That line lives in my bones. Because it's my family's story too. North India. Partition. Everything material -- gone. What remained was the conviction that if you show up and work, the universe cannot keep you down forever.

Two Kalpanas, decades apart, in fields that depend entirely on imagination to function. One reached the stars. One brought India to the moon's south pole where no one else had ventured.

Kalpana creates reality. This isn't a metaphor.

Every invention, every migration, every career pivot, every business -- it began as an image in someone's mind before it became a fact in the world. The family that survived partition and rebuilt. The girl from Karnal who became an astronaut. The scientist who landed a spacecraft where no human instrument had touched before.

The most powerful asset you possess isn't your network or your resume. It's your capacity to imagine something that doesn't yet exist and refuse to let go of that vision.

What reality is your imagination building right now?

### Post 6: Empty Your Cup

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 6**

I once built a team that I loved. Hired them, coached them, fought for their promotions, stayed up on calls across time zones troubleshooting their problems. That team was my identity.

When it was time to move on, I couldn't.

Not because a better opportunity wasn't available. Because I was still full. Full of attachment to what I'd built, full of stories about what we'd accomplish next, full of the version of myself that existed only in relation to that team.

My wife, with the clarity that only someone who loves you can have, said five words: "Your mission with that team is over."

I heard her. But I didn't listen. Not then.

In 2005, I made a career move that should have been exciting. Instead, I went with half a heart. My body relocated but my mind stayed behind, running "what if" scenarios about the team and the life I'd left. I poured new chai into a cup that was still full of yesterday's tea.

It tasted exactly as bad as you'd expect.

Years later, I met someone who had left a 14-year career. For six months after leaving, they struggled. Not with finding new work -- with letting go of the old. Then, slowly, peace came. They had finally emptied the cup.

Here's what I've learnt: it's not enough to empty your cup. You have to rinse it.

Emptying is acknowledging that a chapter ended. Rinsing is processing the grief, the nostalgia, the identity you attached to that chapter. Without rinsing, residue remains. And every new experience gets filtered through the old.

Leave well. Grieve well. Rinse the cup. Then, and only then, pour something new.

जहां प्यार नहीं, वहां व्यापार नहीं. Where there is no love, there is no business -- including the business of starting over.

What cup do you need to rinse before you can fill it again?

### Post 7: We Can Only Give What We Have

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 7**

A friend called me one evening, exhausted. Not from work. From a relationship where she kept giving warmth and getting silence in return.

"Why can't they just be more present?" she asked.

I said something that sounded harsh at first: "Because they don't have presence to give you. You can only give what you have."

She went quiet.

This principle operates everywhere, once you see it.

**In careers:** If your company gives you money but no meaning, no growth, no respect -- it's not being cruel. It's giving you everything it has. If you need something else, you need to go somewhere else. Or build a portfolio of sources. Decouple your needs from a single provider.

**In relationships:** If someone gives you love and warmth, that's because they carry love and warmth inside them. That's a big deal. Don't burden that relationship by demanding they also be your intellectual sparring partner, your therapist, and your adventure companion. Hug that person. Express your gratitude. Build a portfolio of people around you who collectively meet your needs.

**In supporting others:** If someone only shares sadness with you, it's because that's what they're carrying. Not because they want to drain you. They're giving you all they have. Meet them with hope and assurance, not frustration.

The most freeing thing I ever learned was to stop asking people for what they don't possess. It's like being angry at a mango tree for not producing apples. The tree isn't failing. Your expectation is misplaced.

We can only seek greatness, generosity, courage, humor, wisdom, and compassion from those who carry it within them.

And the harder question: What are you giving to others right now? Is that all you have? Or have you been holding back something the world needs?

### Post 8: 71 Bets on People

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 8**

I've written cheques to 71 companies as an angel investor. Seventy-one bets on people I believed in.

I should tell you about the returns. I won't. Because the real returns weren't financial.

Here's what 71 bets taught me about people.

**The pattern that predicts success isn't what you think.** It's not intelligence -- I've backed brilliant founders who couldn't ship a product. It's not funding -- I've seen well-capitalized teams implode in months. It's not the market -- some of the best ideas arrived five years too early and the founders didn't survive the wait.

The pattern is something quieter. I call it **stubborn adaptability.** The willingness to hold the vision while completely reinventing the path. Not pivot-for-the-sake-of-pivot. The kind of flexibility that comes from a founder who is so in love with the problem that they'll learn an entirely new skillset overnight to solve it differently.

**Second pattern: the speed of recovery after a blow.** Every startup gets punched in the face. The ones that survive aren't the ones that don't get hit. They're the ones who get up, adjust the strategy by Tuesday, and call you Wednesday with a new plan. Not denial -- genuine metabolic recovery.

**Third pattern: who they are when no one's watching.** How they treat their team before the good news arrives. How they communicate bad news -- quickly and honestly, or slowly and with spin. Character is the only asset that compounds in every market condition.

Seventy-one bets. Some spectacular successes. Some painful zeros. Most somewhere in between, still fighting. The founders I admire most aren't necessarily the ones who made me money. They're the ones who made me believe that ordinary people with extraordinary resolve can bend reality.

Every investment is ultimately a bet on character. Everything else -- the market, the product, the timing -- is context.

Who are you betting on right now? Including yourself?

### Post 9: From This Moment Forward

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 9**

After the accident in Australia, after the rebuilding, after the nights when starting over felt like carrying stones uphill in the dark -- a teacher's words found me.

Dr. Daisaku Ikeda, the Buddhist philosopher who has shaped my life more than any business book ever could, wrote something I return to almost weekly:

"The causes you make from this moment forward are what matter."

Not the causes you made before the setback. Not the life you had. Not what was fair or unfair. From this moment forward.

There is a ruthless compassion in that phrase. It refuses to let you live in the past. But it also refuses to abandon you there. It says: you still have agency. Right now. Today. The very next thing you do creates a cause for your future.

I have seen this principle work in ways that logic can't fully explain.

The colleague who lost a role she'd prepared years for -- and instead of grieving for months, asked herself "from this moment forward" and built something bigger than the role she lost.

The founder whose company failed publicly -- who took a week, processed it, and started again. Not with bravado. With the quiet understanding that the future isn't determined by the past. It's determined by what you do next.

Buddhism teaches that we don't exist in isolation. Every action sends ripples. The causes you make today -- in how you treat people, how you respond to failure, how you invest your attention -- are seeds. Some bloom quickly. Some take years. But nothing is wasted.

After every setback, every loss, every moment where the ground disappears -- there is only one question worth asking.

Not "why did this happen?"
Not "whose fault is this?"

Just this: What will I do from this moment forward?

That question has saved me more than once.

What will you do from this moment forward?

### Post 10: Determine to Be Not Poor

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 10**

Growing up in North India, we didn't have much. My parents had even less. But I never once saw them turn someone away from our door without offering chai, food, or whatever they had.

They weren't generous despite having little. They were generous because they understood something I only grasped decades later:

Poverty is not only about money.

You can have crores in the bank and be poor in courage. You can have a corner office and be poor in compassion. You can have ten thousand followers and be poor in genuine connection.

We can determine to be not poor.

Not poor in integrity and character.
Not poor in courage and compassion.
Not poor in behaviour and respect.
Not poor in perseverance and hustle.
Not poor in optimism and hope.
Not poor in determination and resolve.

This is not about positive thinking. This is about a daily, conscious choice. The kind of choice my parents made every morning when they woke up with limited means and unlimited generosity.

Dr. Ikeda taught me that true wealth is the richness of the heart. Material wealth without it is just an elaborate decoration on an empty room.

We can determine. Every single day. To be the kind of rich that no market crash, no job loss, no crisis can take away.

What kind of rich will you choose to be today?

### Post 11: Deepti — The World's First Hindi-Speaking Computer

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 11**

In 2002, in a college lab in North India, a computer spoke Hindi for the first time.

Her name was Deepti. I built her. And when she spoke her first word -- a slightly garbled "namaste" through a cheap speaker -- I sat there with my hands shaking. Not because the technology worked. Because something I had carried in my head for months had just stepped into the physical world.

Nobody had asked for a Hindi-speaking computer. No professor assigned it. No company funded it. There was no market research, no TAM calculation, no business case. Just a CS student who thought it was absurd that computers could speak every major Western language but not the language his grandmother dreamed in.

That's the part nobody tells you about building things. The best products don't come from market gaps. They come from a specific, personal indignation. A moment where you look at the world and think: this is incomplete. And it bothers you enough to lose sleep over it.

Deepti was terrible by modern standards. The speech synthesis was rough. The vocabulary was limited. She would mispronounce words in ways that made my friends laugh until they cried. But she worked. A machine spoke Hindi. And the people who heard it -- especially the non-English speakers -- their eyes changed. Not impressed. Recognized. As if someone had finally acknowledged that their language deserved to exist in the future, not just the past.

I didn't know it then, but Deepti taught me everything I would later need to know about entrepreneurship.

Every great product is someone's Deepti. It's something one person built because the absence felt personal. Not because a spreadsheet justified it. Not because a VC said the market was ready. Because the builder couldn't tolerate a world where that thing didn't exist.

The question I carry from that college lab into every boardroom, every investment decision, every product review: Is this someone's Deepti? Or is it someone's spreadsheet?

The difference is everything.

What are you building that nobody asked for -- but the world is incomplete without?

### Post 12: The Partnership — Building Kari by Kriti

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 12**

There's a moment in every marriage where you discover something about your partner that no amount of dating prepared you for.

For me, it was watching Kriti reject a fabric sample for the fourth time in a single afternoon. The vendor was exasperated. I was confused -- the samples looked identical to me. She held up two swatches, pointed at a difference invisible to my eye, and said: "This one has no soul."

That was my introduction to Kari by Kriti. And to a kind of quality obsession that no Google performance review has ever captured.

We built Kari -- artisanal Indian home decor -- together. A Google Director and an artist. An algorithm thinker and a texture thinker. On paper, a perfect complement. In practice, a daily negotiation between two entirely different ways of seeing the world.

At Google, I make decisions with data. Conversion rates. A/B tests. Funnel metrics. Kriti makes decisions with her hands. She touches a product and knows. Not thinks -- knows. She can tell you whether a customer will love something before a single unit ships. I've watched her be right so many times that I've stopped arguing.

Here's what Kari taught me that Google couldn't.

Data tells you what happened. Craft tells you what matters. A customer who buys a hand-block-printed cushion cover isn't optimizing for price or convenience. She's choosing something an artisan's hands made over something a machine stamped out. That choice is irrational by every metric I was trained to worship. And it is the entire business.

Working with your life partner is not collaboration. Collaboration has exit clauses. This doesn't. When we disagree about the business -- and we disagree often -- we can't escalate to a manager. We have to sit with the tension until one of us sees what the other sees. That's a skill no corporate training teaches.

The artisan's hand and the algorithm serve different gods. One serves efficiency. The other serves meaning. The best products -- the ones people keep, recommend, build a home around -- somehow serve both.

Kriti taught me that. Not in a meeting. Over rejected fabric samples and cold chai.

What has someone outside your professional world taught you about quality that your industry couldn't?

### Post 13: What Buddhism Taught a Google Director

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 13**

Every morning at 5:30 AM, before a single Slack notification, before the world starts its demands, I sit in front of a scroll and chant.

Nam-myoho-renge-kyo. Over and over. For about thirty minutes.

If you'd told the version of me from fifteen years ago that this would be my most important daily practice -- more important than exercise, more important than email, more important than any morning routine a productivity influencer has ever sold -- I would have been skeptical.

This isn't a religious post. I'm not here to convert anyone. What I want to share is what a 13th-century Japanese monk named Nichiren understood about human potential that most modern leadership frameworks are still catching up to.

The core teaching of the Lotus Sutra, which sits at the heart of Nichiren Buddhism, is breathtakingly simple: every single person, without exception, possesses unlimited potential. Not some people. Not the talented ones. Not the ones with the right background or the right breaks. Everyone. The difference between a person who transforms their life and one who doesn't isn't capacity. It's whether that capacity is latent or manifest.

That idea changed how I lead.

When I look at someone on my team who's struggling, I don't see a performance problem. I see latent potential that hasn't found its conditions yet. My job isn't to evaluate them. My job is to create the environment where what's already inside them can emerge.

When I sit across from a founder who's been rejected by thirty investors, I don't see a failing business. I see someone whose potential is being tested by whether they'll keep going. The ones who do -- the ones who chant or pray or simply refuse to accept the current reality as final -- they're the ones who bend the world.

Dr. Daisaku Ikeda, the Buddhist philosopher who has shaped my life most profoundly, wrote: "A great human revolution in just a single individual will help achieve a change in the destiny of a nation and further, will enable a change in the destiny of all humankind."

I chant every morning because I believe that. Not as philosophy. As physics.

What daily practice grounds you before the noise begins?

### Post 14: The 71st Bet

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 14**

The call came at 11:47 PM on a Tuesday. A founder I'd backed -- I'll call him Arjun -- was sitting in his car in a parking lot, engine off, unable to drive home.

He'd just been told by his co-founder that she was leaving. His lead investor had pulled the term sheet for the next round. His runway was eleven weeks. He had fourteen employees who trusted him.

He didn't call for advice. He called because he needed someone to sit with him in the dark for a few minutes. So I did. On the phone, saying nothing useful, just being there while he breathed.

That call is worth more to me than any return I've ever made as an angel investor.

Seventy-one companies. I wrote about the patterns in an earlier post -- stubborn adaptability, speed of recovery, character when no one's watching. But what I didn't write about were the humans behind those patterns.

The founder who pivoted four times. Her first idea was a logistics platform. Then edtech. Then healthtech. Then, almost by accident, she stumbled into the problem she was actually born to solve. Each pivot cost her something -- investors who lost patience, team members who left, months of runway burned. But she never lost the thread. She wasn't pivoting away from failure. She was pivoting toward clarity. She's now building one of the most interesting companies in her space.

The founder who had to shut down. This one stays with me. He gathered his team, told them the truth -- money was gone, the product hadn't found its market, it was over. Then he spent his last eight weeks helping every single employee find their next role. He wrote recommendations. He made introductions. He answered calls at midnight from anxious team members. He treated the ending with the same integrity he'd brought to the beginning.

Some of my 71 bets made money. Some went to zero. Most are still in the fight, somewhere between hope and proof. But every single one taught me something about what people are capable of when they care about something more than themselves.

The 71st bet wasn't on a company. It was on the belief that ordinary people with extraordinary resolve can change things.

Whose midnight call would you answer?

### Post 15: The Loudest Silence

**Awakening, Assets & Access | Part 15**

There is a moment that happens almost every evening in our home that I've never described to anyone.

My son walks into the room, looks at me, and places his hand on my chest. Not my hand. Not my shoulder. My chest. Right where the heartbeat is. He holds it there for a few seconds. Then he walks away.

No words. No explanation. No context. Just a hand on a heartbeat, and then gone.

I have sat in rooms where the most articulate people in technology argued for hours and communicated nothing. Where slides were polished and meaning was absent. Where language was used not to reveal but to obscure -- to sound smart, to hedge, to protect.

My son has never spoken a word. And he has never once failed to communicate exactly what he means.

We use the word "intelligence" so casually in this industry. Artificial intelligence. Business intelligence. Emotional intelligence. We throw it around like we know what it means. I've spent twenty years in technology, and I'm less sure of the definition now than when I started. Because I live with someone who would score zero on every standardized measure of intelligence that exists, and who understands things about human connection that I am still trying to learn.

He knows who in the room is anxious before anyone has said a word. He gravitates toward sadness -- not to fix it, but to sit beside it. He has no interest in performance, status, or pretense. He operates in a frequency that most of us have been trained out of hearing.

Silence is not the absence of communication. It is communication with the noise removed.

The boardroom runs on words. Decks. Emails. Talking points rehearsed in the shower. My son runs on something older and more honest. Presence. Touch. The weight of showing up without an agenda.

I used to worry about what he would become. What milestones he would hit. What version of success he could access. I don't anymore. Not because I've given up. Because he redefined the question.

He will not write code or close deals or give keynotes. He will do something harder. He will walk into a room and make it more human just by being in it. He already does.

Every person I've met who changed my life did it the same way. Not with their words. With their presence.

What is the loudest silence in your life trying to tell you?
