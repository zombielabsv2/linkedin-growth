## Post 1: Are You Tiring Yourself Out?

**Date:** October 22, 2021 (edited)
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rahuljindal_are-you-tiring-yourself-out-our-brain-is-activity-6857341959908937728-DDmk
**Likes:** 79 | **Comments:** 8

Are you tiring yourself out? Our brain is a thought producing machine. It's a machine. Receiving multisensory stimuli, mixing it up with past memories, formulating projections into the future and thereby guiding actions to respond accordingly - every single moment.

I used to think that the sage advice of 'master your mind' meant to control your thoughts! Only to realise that such mastery means giving yourself the freedom and courage to *choose* which momentary thought you act on and which you will *choose* to let pass.

You let a thought pass and the machine dutifully produces the next one - hopefully a different one, assuming you change your stimuli - look elsewhere, walk (away), breathe deeply, bring back happy memories, and other such conscious actions. Voila!

We can't control the thoughts but we can choose what to do with any thought. 'Choicefulness'! How empowering!

As the great Viktor E Frankel said, 'Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response.'

The post concludes by applying this principle to organizational strategy, referencing Prof Saral Mukherjee's concept that strategy involves knowing which doors to close.

## Post 5: Action Is A Great Distraction

**Date:** January 27, 2023
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rahuljindal_keepmovingforward-activity-7024696554787663872-rax1
**Likes:** 78 | **Comments:** 5

Action is a great distraction. The 2020s were meant to be a period of significant advancement, joy and unprecedented experiences. Humanity got the unprecedented experiences alright - though not the kinds anyone imagined. 2020-present: doesn't sound much fun, does it? Anxiety is widespread, whatever maybe the driver for an individual - health, economy, loneliness & isolation, disillusionment - the impact is joylessness, fear and anger. How then is one to cope? No magic bullets and no one size fits all here. What I am doing is:

1. Talk to others: Irrespective of the gravity of one's suffering, no one is uniquely suffering their respective condition. Speaking to others, especially in a similar condition, is powerfully therapeutic. Just getting it out of one's system can be uplifting. Seeing others cope, and sometimes cope better than us, can be a source of encouragement, hope and camaraderie.

2. Help others: There's a saying, 'When you light a lantern for others, your path is also lit.' In tasking ourselves to helping others in a similar situation, we also find ways to help ourselves. We find the energy to do so. We find a network to do it with. If we task ourselves to share 3 high quality ideas with others, we might come up with 10. Those 10 are ours to try.

Yes, everyone copes differently to stressful situations. Action can be a great distraction too. As The Flash says, 'Life is locomotion. If you're not moving, you're not living'.

#keepmovingforward

Professionals often get trapped in observation and contribution roles, sacrificing impact-generating work. "As time spent on counting the trees goes up, it comes at the cost of not seeing the forest."

## Post 12: Career Volume

**Date:** July 3, 2024
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7214185948475969536/
**Likes:** 116 | **Comments:** 10

Career volume: Length x Breadth x Depth

Five pathways for career transitions:

1. Same industry, same function, different company - Requires recruiter outreach, networking, or serendipitous encounters.

2. Same industry, different function, same company - Needs internal postings, internal recruiters, or cross-functional collaboration.

3. Same industry, different function, different company - Higher education like an MBA helps hiring managers view candidates freshly.

4. Different industry, same function - Requires talented recruiters, functional expertise reputation, or advanced degrees with campus recruitment.

5. Different industry, different function - The hardest transition, typically requiring an MBA plus compelling articulation of core value.

Recruiters contact discoverable candidates with strong reputations. Networks help those who previously received unconditional support. Internal opportunities emerge from demonstrating significant collaborative value. Serendipity favors those who create good causes and help less privileged individuals.

Career volume expands through increased length (years worked), depth (executive or expert status), or breadth (multi-functional experience).

## Post 13: Good Manager vs Nice Manager

**Date:** September 25, 2022
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rahuljindal_management-decisions-activity-6979772211431714817-U8RB
**Likes:** 448 | **Comments:** 35

Every single day, and multiple times in a day, you will have a choice to make: to be a good manager or to be a nice manager. Make that choice but let there be no judgement on which one is better - good or nice.

Good managers prioritize long-term growth, build systems and processes, earn respect over years, and challenge employees to develop skills. Their payoff "matures in years and decades."

Nice managers focus on immediate comfort, strengthen relationships and perceptions, gain popularity, and provide easy direction. Their payoff "matures in weeks and months."

Key distinctions:
- "Good may be remembered, privately. Nice will be celebrated, publicly."
- "Good will grow in their character. Nice will grow in their charter."
- "Good is centred around purposes. Nice is centered around persons."

You can be good. Nice can be you. Which one will you be? Did you say, 'it depends'? Yes, it does.

## Post 15: First 90 Days As A Manager

**Date:** January 5, 2023
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rahuljindal_first-90-days-as-a-manager-1-do-observe-activity-7016687059218161664-HzRL
**Likes:** 233 | **Comments:** 11

First 90 Days as a Manager:

1. Observe organizational culture - Rather than rushing to implement changes, new managers should study how work gets accomplished, how achievements are recognized, and what level of authenticity is acceptable in the workplace.

2. Create a personal "user guide" - Managers should communicate their working preferences and encourage team members to share theirs. "We are coaching, supporting and managing people - not machines," noting individual needs vary and evolve over time.

3. Read "The First 90 Days" by Michael Watkins - Advocates preparation and intentionality rather than improvisation, stating teams deserve thoughtful leadership.

4. Develop cross-cultural sensitivity - For global teams with diverse nationalities, managers should pursue training and coaching. Cautions against assuming "the same narrative or style that worked while managing a homogenous nationality team will work."

## Post 16: Dealing With An Underappreciated Team Member

**Date:** January 10, 2023
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rahuljindal_q-how-should-i-deal-with-my-team-member-activity-7018557264756187136-yKSM
**Likes:** 134 | **Comments:** 6

Q: How should I deal with my team member who is feeling underappreciated and insecure?

A: Some thoughts:

1. Nearly everyone experiences feelings of underappreciation and insecurity at various points in their career.

2. However, this requires action. "Your team member's feelings are your business. Not *only* your business but your business nonetheless."

3. Discover their preferred appreciation method - immediate feedback, public recognition, monetary rewards, or visibility to leadership. Ask directly rather than assume.

4. Examine whether favoritism (real or perceived) might explain their insecurity, even unintentionally.

5. Consider whether their role carries equivalent importance as peers in your assessment and your supervisor's perspective.

6. Understand their broader life context. Work might represent their sole stable element during personal difficulties, justifying increased emotional needs.

7. Challenge beliefs about rationing appreciation. "Infinite amount of appreciation can be produced. By you. Don't ration appreciation!" People don't become spoiled - they gain energy for demanding work.

8. Monitor how visibly you recognize their achievements versus peers' accomplishments in emails and reports. Inconsistent visibility breeds insecurity.

Key insight: Environmental factors - not inherent predisposition - create insecurity. Managers can reshape workplace environments constructively through genuine recognition.

## Post 17: Managing A New Hire From Another Org

**Date:** January 13, 2023
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rahuljindal_q-how-do-i-manage-this-person-that-i-have-activity-7019563640743727104-NBun
**Likes:** 100 | **Comments:** 17

Q: How do I manage this person that I have hired from another org?

A: Five key points:

1. Understand their previous environment - manager style, org culture, communication preferences (oral vs. written), decision-making pace, etc. Compare and contrast their old and new contexts.

2. Avoid abrupt changes without support. "Help them deal with it at a digestible rate."

3. Articulate goals and ask their approach. Begin with trust rather than skepticism to minimize long-term management overhead.

4. Showcase concrete examples of your standards for both outcomes and methods. Eliminate guesswork about expectations.

5. Publicly recognize attributes you want replicated. Peer observation drives motivation.

Closing philosophy: "An organization's culture is who they hire, promote and fire. Nothing more, nothing less." Stated values mean nothing without aligned practices in hiring, performance management, and separation policies. Demonstrate your beliefs through actions.
