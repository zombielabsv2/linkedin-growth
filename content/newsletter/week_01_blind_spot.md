# The Blind Spot in Every AI Strategy

*The Seven Conversations | Issue #1*

---

Every enterprise AI strategy I've seen makes the same mistake.

It starts with IT. It lives in IT. And it dies in IT.

The CTO picks a platform. The data team builds a model. A pilot gets launched. The board gets a presentation with the words "AI-powered" on every slide. And somewhere in the building, the General Counsel's office is still reviewing contracts by hand, the CFO's team is running forecasts on spreadsheets from 2019, and the supply chain team is reacting to disruptions that AI could have predicted two weeks ago.

The mistake isn't technical. It's structural.

## The Seven Languages Problem

AI transformation isn't one conversation. It's seven different conversations happening in seven different languages.

The **CLO** talks about risk, liability, and compliance. The **CFO** talks about ROI, scenarios, and cost reduction. The **CHRO** talks about workforce impact, reskilling, and change management. The **CTO** talks about platforms, architecture, and infrastructure. The **COO** talks about process redesign and operational efficiency. The **CMO** talks about personalization and customer experience. And the **CEO** is trying to synthesize all of it into something the board can understand.

Here's the problem: in most enterprises, these seven conversations happen in different rooms, on different timelines, using different vocabularies. The CLO doesn't know what the CTO just deployed. The CFO doesn't know the CHRO is piloting AI for workforce planning. The supply chain team has no idea that the marketing team's demand signals could improve their forecast by 30%.

Nobody is translating.

## Why This Gap Exists

It exists because of how enterprises organized themselves for the last 50 years. Functions were designed as vertical silos — each with its own budget, its own metrics, its own leadership. That structure worked for efficiency. It fails for transformation.

AI is inherently cross-functional. A contract review AI (Legal) needs data governance (IT), risk assessment (Compliance), change management (HR), and budget approval (Finance). Deploying it in one silo is easy. Making it actually work requires five.

The consulting firms know this. McKinsey, BCG, and Accenture — who collectively do $7+ billion annually in AI consulting — all say the same thing: 70% of AI transformation success is people, process, and culture. 30% is technology.

But most enterprises still treat it as a technology project with some change management sprinkled on top.

## What the Winners Do Differently

I've spent the last decade watching how large enterprises absorb new technology. The pattern is remarkably consistent.

The companies that fail deploy AI *into* their existing structure. They pick a function, build a use case, celebrate the pilot, and wonder why it doesn't scale.

The companies that succeed deploy AI *across* their structure. They don't ask "which function gets AI first?" They ask "which business outcome requires multiple functions to work together, and how does AI make that coordination faster?"

That reframe — from function-first to outcome-first — is the difference between an AI project and an AI transformation.

## What to Do Monday Morning

1. **Map the seven conversations.** Literally list who in your organization is making AI decisions for each function — Strategy, Architecture, Talent, Risk, Numbers, Operations, Customer. If any of those roles are empty or defaulting to IT, you've found your blind spot.

2. **Find the orphaned signals.** What data does one function generate that another function could use? Marketing's demand signals for supply chain. HR's attrition predictions for finance. Legal's contract patterns for sales. These cross-functional data flows are where AI creates the most value — and where most enterprises have zero infrastructure.

3. **Ask who translates.** If your AI strategy doesn't have someone who can sit in a room with the General Counsel AND the CTO AND the CFO and be credible with all three, you have a strategy without a translator. That role — the AI Translator — is the scarcest talent in enterprise AI. More on that in a future issue.
