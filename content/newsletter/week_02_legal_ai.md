# What Happens When Legal Teams Discover AI

*The Seven Conversations | Issue #2*

---

The legal team is usually the last to adopt AI and the first to regulate it.

That irony contains everything you need to know about why enterprise AI transformation is harder than the technology vendors suggest.

## The 6,000 Contract Problem

I spoke with a General Counsel who told me her team was reviewing 6,000 contracts a year. Her best associate could do 12 a day with high accuracy. The math was brutal — they were permanently 18 months behind on contract reviews. Not because of incompetence. Because the volume of business had outgrown the capacity of the team, and hiring more associates was both expensive and slow.

She didn't start with a vendor demo. She started with a question: *"What if we could review every contract the day it arrives?"*

That question reframed the entire project. They weren't looking for a tool that reads contracts faster. They were redesigning how legal work flows through the organization.

Here's what they built:

**Stage 1: AI-powered intake.** Every contract gets an automated first pass. The AI extracts key terms — liability caps, indemnification clauses, auto-renewal terms, jurisdiction — and flags deviations from the company's standard positions.

**Stage 2: Exception routing.** Instead of associates reading every contract, they review only the exceptions the AI flagged. A contract that matches the company's standards flows through automatically. A contract with unusual indemnification language gets routed to a senior associate.

**Stage 3: Pattern detection.** Over time, the AI builds a map of which counterparties consistently push on which clauses. This turns contract review from reactive (reviewing what arrives) to predictive (knowing what to expect before the contract even comes in).

Turnaround went from 3 weeks to 3 days. The same team, doing fundamentally different work.

## The Harder Conversation

But here's what nobody talks about in the case study version of this story.

The hard part wasn't deploying the AI. It was the conversation with the partners and senior associates who had built their entire career on the skill of slow, careful, thorough reading. Their professional identity was "I am the person who catches what everyone else misses." And now a machine was doing the first pass on their craft.

Telling someone that their craft is now a commodity is a conversation that requires more emotional courage than any technology deployment. It is fundamentally a leadership conversation, not a technology conversation. And it is the conversation that determines whether an AI deployment succeeds or creates a resentful team that quietly sabotages the rollout.

The GC handled it by reframing. She didn't say "AI is replacing contract review." She said "AI is freeing you to do the work you went to law school for." The associates who spent 80% of their time on routine clause-by-clause reading were now spending 80% of their time on judgment calls, exception analysis, and strategic counsel. The ones who adapted thrived. A few didn't.

## Three Patterns in Legal AI That Actually Works

**1. Start with volume, not complexity.** Contract review before litigation strategy. The reason is simple: volume problems have clear metrics (throughput, turnaround time, error rate), clear data (contracts are structured documents), and clear ROI. Complexity problems (litigation prediction, regulatory interpretation) are harder to measure and harder to validate.

**2. Reframe the role.** Associates become "exception analysts," not "document reviewers." This isn't branding. It's a genuine shift in what the work requires. The skills that matter shift from reading comprehension to pattern recognition, judgment under ambiguity, and business risk assessment.

**3. Let Legal own the AI governance charter.** This is the most underrated move in enterprise AI. Legal teams are natural regulators. They think about risk, liability, accountability, and precedent by training. Instead of having Legal resist AI adoption, give them the enterprise-wide AI governance charter. Let them write the rules for how every function uses AI. This turns Legal from the "department of no" into the "department of how."

## Why Legal Might Be Your Most Important AI Function

There's a counterintuitive argument I want to make: the legal function might be the most strategically important function in your entire AI transformation. Not because legal work has the highest ROI for AI (supply chain does — more on that in Issue #5). But because Legal sits at the intersection of risk, governance, and compliance — the three things that slow every other function's AI adoption.

If Legal is blocking AI deployment, every function slows down. If Legal is enabling AI deployment by providing clear governance frameworks, every function speeds up.

The companies where the CLO and the CTO have a strong working relationship are deploying AI 2-3x faster than companies where Legal and IT barely talk. That relationship — the Risk Conversation meeting the Architecture Conversation — is one of the seven conversations that makes or breaks enterprise AI.

## What to Do Monday Morning

1. **Ask your legal team what they're drowning in.** Not what AI they want — what volume problem is breaking them. That's your first use case.

2. **Give Legal the AI governance charter.** Before your next AI deployment in any function, ask: "Has Legal written the rules for how we use AI here?" If not, that's step one.

3. **Map the Legal-IT relationship.** How often do your CLO and CTO actually talk? If the answer is "quarterly" or "when there's a problem," you have a seven-conversations gap that's slowing down your entire transformation.
