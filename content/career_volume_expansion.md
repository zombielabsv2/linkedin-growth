## Post 11: The Compound Interest of Staying

**Topic:** When staying is the bold move
**Target:** 270 words

---

A friend pinged me last month. Third job in four years. Each move came with a 30% raise. On paper -- killing it.

Then he said something that stopped me: "I keep starting over. I have no sponsors. Nobody goes to bat for me. I'm always the new guy proving himself."

He had optimized for the 18-month itch. And it had cost him the 5-year dividend.

Here's what nobody tells you about staying:

The relationships that form in year 2 are transactional. The relationships that form in year 5 are transformational. Your manager in year 1 evaluates you. Your sponsor in year 5 advocates for you -- in rooms you'll never enter.

The institutional knowledge you accumulate by year 4 makes you the person people call when things break. Not because of your title. Because you've seen the movie before. You know where the bodies are buried.

I call this **The Compound Interest of Staying.** Just like financial compounding, career compounding is back-loaded. The first few years feel slow. The returns stack up later -- in trust, in access, in influence, in the roles that never get posted.

Rapid job-hopping looks like ambition. Often, it's impatience wearing ambition's clothes. Every switch resets your compounding clock to zero.

This doesn't mean stay in a toxic environment. It doesn't mean tolerate stagnation. But if the soil is good and the sun is there -- the question isn't "should I leave?" The question is "have I harvested what this place can give me?"

Sometimes, staying is the boldest career move you can make.

Where in your career did staying longer pay a dividend you didn't expect?

## Post 12: The Invisible Resume

**Topic:** The resume nobody sees but everyone reads
**Target:** 260 words

---

I once recommended a former colleague for a senior role at a company where I knew the hiring VP. No interview prep. No resume review. Just one line from me: "She's the person you want in a crisis. I've seen it."

She got the job. The one that was never posted.

That's the Invisible Resume at work.

Your actual resume is two pages. Your **Invisible Resume** is infinite. It's the way you handle pressure when the deadline moves up by two weeks. It's how you treat the intern, the receptionist, the vendor -- people with zero power over your career. It's whether you deliver when nobody's watching and no one will know if you cut corners.

Most importantly, it's your reputation in rooms you're not in.

I've been in hundreds of hiring and promotion discussions. The stated resume gets you the meeting. The Invisible Resume gets you the outcome. I've seen brilliant resumes get vetoed by one person saying, "Technically strong, but difficult to work with." And I've seen average resumes get championed by someone saying, "This person raised the bar of everyone around them."

The Invisible Resume is being written every single day. Every interaction is a line item. Every crisis is a performance review. Every moment when you think no one is paying attention -- someone is.

You can't attach it to a job application. But it arrives before you do.

Perceptions matter. They always have.

What would your Invisible Resume say about you -- if you could read it?

## Post 13: The Portfolio Career

**Topic:** Diversified lives are not unfocused lives
**Target:** 280 words

---

A well-meaning uncle at a family gathering once told me: "Beta, you're doing too many things. Pick one and focus."

I was a Google Director running AI transformation. I was also building Kari by Kriti, an artisanal home decor brand, with my wife. I was angel investing in startups. I was writing on LinkedIn. I was advising early-stage founders.

By his framework, I was scattered. By mine, I was diversified.

There's a difference between scattered and diversified that most people miss.

**Scattered** = many things, no depth in any, driven by FOMO, each one competing for the same energy. You're spinning plates and dreading the crash.

**Diversified** = multiple bets, each feeding a different part of you, each strengthening the others. Your corporate job funds your risk appetite. Your side project keeps your creativity alive. Your investing sharpens your pattern recognition. Your writing forces clarity of thought.

I call this **The Portfolio Career.** Borrowed from investing -- because the logic is identical. You don't put your entire net worth in one stock. Why would you put your entire identity in one role?

The test for whether you're scattered or diversified is simple: does each activity make the others better? My investing makes me a sharper operator at Google. My writing makes me a better mentor. Kari by Kriti taught me things about customer obsession that no corporate playbook ever could.

Not everyone needs a portfolio career. But if you feel the pull toward multiple things, don't let the "just focus" crowd shame you into a single lane.

Some people are highways. Others are river deltas. Both reach the ocean.

Is your career a single stock -- or a portfolio?

## Post 14: The Mentorship Myth

**Topic:** Build a constellation, not a pedestal
**Target:** 270 words

---

"Can you be my mentor?"

I've received this message hundreds of times. And every time, I feel a quiet unease. Not because I don't want to help. But because the question itself is built on a myth.

The myth is this: somewhere out there is a wise, senior person who will take you under their wing, guide your career, open doors, and tell you what to do when life gets confusing. Find that person. Problem solved.

It almost never works that way.

The best mentorship I've received didn't come from one person on a pedestal. It came from **a constellation.**

My colleague who modeled what flawless execution looks like -- never gave me advice, but I watched and learned more than any formal mentor could teach. A friend who asks brutally hard questions every time I'm about to make a comfortable choice. Authors I'll never meet -- Nassim Taleb, who taught me about antifragility. Naval Ravikant, who reframed wealth. A founder who is only two years ahead of me, not twenty -- close enough that her struggles mirror mine.

Each person in the constellation gives me something different. No single person could give me everything.

Here's the other problem with "find a mentor": it puts the burden on someone else. **Build a constellation** puts the agency back on you. Read voraciously. Observe colleagues who are great at what you're weak at. Ask specific questions, not "guide me." Give before you ask. Relationships compound -- but only when both sides feel the exchange.

Stop searching for The Mentor. There isn't one.

Start building the constellation. There are many.

Who are the five people in your constellation right now -- and what does each one give you?

## Post 15: Your Career is a Story, Not a Ladder

**Topic:** Tying the series together
**Target:** 290 words

---

We've been lied to about careers.

The lie goes like this: there's a ladder. It goes up. You climb it. Each rung is a promotion, a bigger title, a higher number. The person at the top wins.

But the most extraordinary careers I've witnessed -- in 25+ years of mentoring, hiring, and building teams -- don't look like ladders at all. They look like stories.

Stories go sideways. They go backwards. They take detours through unexpected doors. A computer science degree leads to intellectual property, which leads to Australia, which leads to financial services, which leads to Google. An Uber driver in the Bay Area teaches you more about resilience in 20 minutes than a leadership offsite does in three days. A freak accident shows you that you can build it all over again.

Ladders have one direction. Stories have chapters.

Some chapters are triumphant -- the promotion, the standing ovation, the bet that paid off. Some chapters are brutal -- the phone call you hung up on, the winter that wouldn't end, the gap that felt infinite. And some chapters only make sense ten years later, when you look back and see the thread connecting everything.

That thread? It's your **Career Volume.**

**Length** -- the stamina to keep showing up, chapter after chapter.
**Breadth** -- the courage to explore sideways, not just upward.
**Depth** -- the discipline to go deep enough that your work carries weight.

A ladder is someone else's structure. A story is yours.

You are the author. Not your company. Not your industry. Not the economy. You.

The question was never "what's the next rung?"

It was always "what's the next chapter?"

What chapter are you writing right now?
