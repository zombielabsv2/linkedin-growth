**Life after AI | The Anthropocenic Turn**

We spent three centuries making humans more productive. Then we built the thing that doesn't need us to be productive at all.

Sam Jacobs calls this the "Anthropocenic" era. His argument is five words long and it hasn't left my head in weeks: "The most important natural resource is humans — and we're running out."

Not running out of people. Running out of economic relevance for people.

Think about it. The Industrial Revolution valued your hands. The information economy valued your mind. Each wave extracted more from the human organism — and we built entire civilizations around that extraction. We called it "human resources." We measured "human capital." We optimized "human productivity."

Resources are extracted and consumed. That was the logic. And it worked — as long as the economy needed what humans could produce.

AI breaks that logic. Not partially. Completely.

For the first time, we've built systems that can produce without us. The natural resource isn't depleted because humans got worse. It's depleted because human capability is no longer scarce.

This isn't an unemployment crisis. It's a purpose crisis.

If we defined ourselves by what we produce — and three hundred years of economic architecture says we did — then what happens when production doesn't need us?

I've been sitting with this question for months. It touches everything — identity, income, meaning, relationships, education, governance. It's the thread that connects every chapter of the book I'm writing.

The Anthropocenic era doesn't end the human story. But it ends the chapter where the story was about what we could do.

The next chapter is about who we choose to become.

What are humans FOR — when machines do the productive work?
