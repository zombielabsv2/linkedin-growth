## Post 1: The Career Volume Equation

**Topic:** Length x Breadth x Depth
**Target:** 250 words

---

In school, we learned that Volume = Length x Breadth x Depth.

Turns out, careers work the same way.

A friend called me last week. 17 years in one function at one company. Stellar performer. Just got passed over for a role she wanted badly. "I have so much experience," she said. I asked her: "Experience in what dimensions?"

She had Length -- years and years of it. But almost no Breadth across functions. And Depth? She'd stopped going deeper five years ago when the role became comfortable.

Her career had become a thin, long line. Not a volume.

Here's the framework I keep coming back to:

**Length** = years worked, stamina, longevity. The marathon dimension.
**Breadth** = functions touched, industries crossed, geographies navigated. The versatility dimension.
**Depth** = how far you've gone in your domain -- executive presence, thought leadership, mastery. The gravity dimension.

A 20-year career with no breadth is a line.
A career across five industries with no depth is a surface.
A deep expert who burns out in four years is a dot.

Volume requires all three.

The beautiful thing? You don't need to maximize all three simultaneously. At different stages, you expand different dimensions. Early career -- go deep. Mid-career -- go wide. Late career -- go long and share.

But you need to be intentional about which dimension you're expanding right now. Otherwise, someone else is choosing for you.

Which dimension of your career volume are you actively expanding today?

## Post 2: The Wrong Race

**Topic:** The "youngest in the room" trap
**Target:** 250 words

---

"How do I stay the youngest person in the room?"

A mentee asked me this with the intensity of someone preparing for battle. I told him something he didn't want to hear.

Don't be in that race.

I get it. In your 20s and early 30s, being the youngest VP, the youngest director, the youngest anything -- it feels like proof that you belong. I chased that too. The dopamine of being "ahead" is intoxicating.

But here's what nobody tells you about that race: the finish line keeps moving. And the impatience it breeds? That's the real career killer. Not being "behind."

Impatience makes you switch roles before the compounding kicks in. It makes you optimize for title over learning. It makes work a comparison sport -- and comparison is a team sport's worst enemy.

Instead of asking "Am I the youngest?" try asking these:

What are the 5 things I need to feel happy, successful, and fulfilled? Not the Instagram version. The real list.

Which of those can my current role actually give me? Painful truth: no single role gives you everything. Especially as you grow senior.

For what's missing -- where else can I find it? Hobbies, mentoring, communities, side projects, faith.

When your A through E are well-identified and well-addressed, the obsession with being youngest evaporates. You stop running someone else's race.

The people with the most enduring careers aren't the ones who arrived earliest. They're the ones who kept building after everyone else got tired.

What race are you actually running?

## Post 3: They Said You Can Change Only One

**Topic:** Breaking career transition rules
**Target:** 270 words

---

They said you can change only one of these at a time: role, industry, or country.

I changed all three. Simultaneously.

Here's the backstory. I had a computer science degree, an MBA, and years in intellectual property -- a niche that most people couldn't even spell correctly. I wanted to move to Australia, switch to financial services, and shift from leadership to an individual contributor role.

Every career advisor said: pick one. Change your country but stay in your industry. Change your function but stay in your geography. The "conventional wisdom" was clear -- changing multiple variables is a recipe for rejection.

So I did something different. I spent six months in deep introspection -- what I call the Manthan, the churning. And I distilled my entire career into one transferable truth:

"I work with senior CXO-level stakeholders, understand what they need in their business, and develop solutions that help them succeed."

That's it. That was the thread that connected IP to financial services, India to Australia, leadership to individual contribution. Not industry knowledge. Not functional expertise. The ability to understand a business leader's problem and build something useful.

That two-minute articulation, born from six months of churning, unlocked conversations that no resume could have.

The "change only one" rule exists because most people can't articulate what transfers. Not because multiple changes are impossible. The constraint isn't the market -- it's your clarity about what you actually bring to the table.

What's your transferable core? Not your title. Not your industry. The thing that works anywhere.

## Post 4: The Manthan

**Topic:** Deep introspection before big moves
**Target:** 260 words

---

In Indian mythology, the Manthan is the great churning of the ocean -- gods and demons pulling a serpent back and forth around a mountain until treasures emerge from the chaos.

My career's most important turning point looked nothing like a promotion or a job offer. It looked like six months of doing nothing visible.

I was between roles. The temptation was to spray resumes everywhere, say yes to the first reasonable offer, stay busy. Instead, I chose to churn.

Every morning, I wrote. What do I actually enjoy doing? What am I genuinely good at versus what I've just been doing by default? What would I regret not trying? What story do I tell myself about my career that might not be true?

Most of those mornings produced garbage. Repetitive loops. Self-pity dressed as analysis. But slowly, like sediment settling in water, clarity emerged.

I realized my real skill wasn't in any specific domain. It was in sitting across from a business leader, understanding what kept them up at night, and translating that into a solution they could act on. Industry didn't matter. Function didn't matter.

That clarity -- hard-won, not downloaded from a career quiz -- became the foundation for every major move afterward.

Here's what I've noticed: the people who make the sharpest career pivots aren't the most networked or the most credentialed. They're the ones who did the Manthan. Who sat with the discomfort of not knowing, long enough for something true to surface.

Are you churning -- or just spinning?

## Post 5: Gap vs Gain

**Topic:** The burnout equation
**Target:** 270 words

---

A colleague sat across from me last Tuesday. On paper -- thriving. Great title, good compensation, respected by peers. In person -- hollow.

"I feel like I'm never enough," she said.

I asked her one question: "Tell me five things you've accomplished in the last three years that you're genuinely proud of."

Silence. Then, slowly, reluctantly -- five remarkable things poured out. A product launch that changed a business unit. A team she built from scratch. A mentee who got promoted. Moving her parents to a better home. Running a half marathon after a knee injury.

She had never paused to count them.

This is the Gap vs Gain problem.

**Gap = Future minus Present.** Where I want to be versus where I am. The ever-expanding to-do list. The next promotion, the next milestone, the next zero in the bank account.

**Gain = Present minus Past.** Where I am versus where I started. What I've already survived, built, and become.

Most high achievers are Gap-obsessed. It's the fuel that got us out of middle-class constraints, year after year, decade after decade. But here's the trap: the Gap is a mirage. There will always be more. Always.

Single-minded pursuit of the Gap = recipe for burnout.
Accounting for the Gain = recipe for renewal.

Just like Earth needs both rotation and revolution to sustain life, you need both the Gap and the Gain to sustain yours. Determination for the future and gratitude for the past. Becoming and being.

You are the person you have to live with your entire life. That person deserves some applause.

When did you last account for your Gain?

## Post 6: Raising the Stakes

**Topic:** Self-sabotage story
**Target:** 250 words

---

In 2005, I hung up on the one phone call that could have changed my career.

I was between jobs. A recruiter had called about a prestigious research firm opening their India office. Senior leadership role. This was it. The big one. I told myself: "This is now or never."

The firm's leader, Krishna, was going to call me at 7 PM. I started checking the landline's dial tone at 5 PM. Every five minutes. Is it working? What if it's broken? What if he already called and I missed it?

By 7 PM, I was a wreck. By 7:15, I was convinced it wasn't happening. When the phone finally rang at 7:30 and a warm voice said, "Hi Rahul, my name is Krishna. How are you doing?" -- I replied:

"I am good. Thanks. Bye."

And hung up.

I wish I was joking.

The only explanation I have, twenty years later, is that I had raised the stakes so high that my nervous system couldn't handle the actual moment. I had turned a conversation into a verdict on my entire life. And my body chose escape.

This is what self-sabotage looks like. Not dramatic acts of destruction. Just quiet moments where the pressure we manufacture exceeds our capacity to perform.

It's never now or never. Tomorrow is another day. The sun will rise again.

The next time you catch yourself treating an opportunity like your last chance at oxygen -- pause. Breathe. Lower the stakes. You'll show up better.

What stakes have you unnecessarily raised for yourself?

## Post 7: Speaking Someone Else's Language

**Topic:** The Mr. Mahindra reframe
**Target:** 260 words

---

Imagine you're in an elevator with Anand Mahindra. You have 60 seconds. You start talking about your "cross-functional transformation initiatives" and "platform engineering expertise."

His eyes glaze over in three seconds.

Now imagine you say: "I help businesses get to market 40% faster and save Rs. 200 crores annually in operational costs."

Different conversation entirely.

I learned this the hard way. For years, I described my work in the vocabulary of my industry. Cloud migrations. Digital transformation. Agile methodologies. When I spoke to people in tech, they nodded. When I spoke to people outside tech, I got polite smiles and zero callbacks.

The breakthrough came when I realized: every business leader, whether they run an auto company or a hospital chain, cares about exactly four things.

1. Growing revenue
2. Reducing cost
3. Moving faster
4. Keeping stakeholders happy

That's it. Everything else is implementation detail.

The moment I started translating my work into those four languages, doors opened that I didn't even know existed. Not because my skills changed -- but because my audience could finally hear me.

This applies beyond job interviews. Pitching an internal project? Don't talk about the technology. Talk about which of the four it serves. Negotiating a raise? Don't list your activities. Quantify your impact in terms the CFO cares about.

Drop your industry's jargon. Speak the language of the person across the table.

The best communicators aren't the most articulate. They're the most translatable.

Whose language do you need to learn to speak?

## Post 8: Choosing Environments

**Topic:** Expectation mismatch to exceptional match
**Target:** 270 words

---

In raising our son, my wife and I kept asking: "Is our child fit for this school?"

The question that changed everything was the reverse: "Is this school fit for our child?"

That one reframe -- from fitting yourself into environments to finding environments that fit you -- applies to careers more than we admit.

I've watched talented people wither in prestigious companies. Not because they lacked skill but because the environment bred anxiety, not acceleration. The hours, the politics, the passive-aggressive emails, the culture of performing busyness -- it was slowly poisoning them. And they stayed because the logo on their LinkedIn profile looked good.

I've also watched average performers become extraordinary when they found the right soil. A manager who actually coached. A team that valued contribution over optics. A pace that matched their working rhythm.

The difference between burnout and brilliance is often not about the person. It's about the match.

An environment that breeds anxiety is no good for you -- regardless of the brand name. An environment that breeds growth will make you shine -- even if nobody's heard of the company.

Here's a practical test before you take your next role. Don't just ask: "Can I do this job?" Also ask:

- How does my future manager handle disagreement?
- What happens to people who fail here?
- Do people leave energized or drained at the end of the day?
- Is the culture stated or actually practiced?

An expectation mismatch will make you burn.
An exceptional match will make you shine.

Shine, don't burn.

Are you in an environment fit for you -- or are you fitting yourself into an environment?

## Post 9: Winter Is the Time to Build

**Topic:** Building during career slumps
**Target:** 280 words

---

No career goes "up and to the right" forever. Mine certainly didn't.

There were seasons where everything clicked -- promotions, recognition, momentum. And there were winters. Painful, quiet stretches where progress felt invisible and self-doubt became a daily visitor.

Here's what nobody puts on LinkedIn: the winters are where the real building happens.

We love attributing the good seasons to our hard work and the bad ones to external forces. But agency means owning both. And the most valuable thing you can do in a career winter is stop trying to force spring -- and start building instead.

**Build faith.** We seek when we suffer. If suffering leads to seeking, then suffering isn't the enemy. Netflix and Instagram will always be there. Let the winter be a time for something deeper.

**Build health.** That exercise routine you sacrificed for late-night emails? That sleep you traded for "giving your best"? Your body kept score. Let this be the time to settle the debt.

**Build relationships.** Thousands of LinkedIn connections. Hundreds of B-school contacts. When did you last have a real conversation with any of them? Dormant relationships are like dormant seeds. Water them.

**Build skills.** When the career is cruising, skill-building takes a back seat. No function and no industry is staying static. Let the winter be your classroom.

**Build a venture.** A side project. A creative outlet. Something that could become an occupation someday -- or simply keep your mind alive.

Winter is not a detour from your career. It's the foundation for the next chapter.

What are you building in your winter?

## Post 10: The 10-Year Question Reframed

**Topic:** Find who you admire, not where you'll be
**Target:** 260 words

---

"Where do you see yourself in 10 years?"

I've been asked this question dozens of times. I've asked it to hundreds of people I've mentored. And after 25+ years of watching careers unfold, I'm convinced it's the wrong question.

The world changes too fast for 10-year predictions. Technology disrupts industries overnight. Pandemics rearrange economies. A single conversation can redirect your entire trajectory. Pretending we can plot coordinates on a decade-long map is comforting but dishonest.

Here's what I ask instead:

**Who do you admire who is 10 years ahead of you?**

Not a celebrity. Someone you've actually observed. Maybe a senior colleague whose life seems balanced. A founder who builds with integrity. A professor who lights up when teaching. A friend's parent who retired with peace, not regret.

Now ask: what specifically about their life do I admire?

Is it their wealth? Their influence? Their relationships? Their calm? Their health? Their sense of purpose? Their gratitude?

That list -- honest, specific, personal -- is your real answer to the 10-year question. Not a job title. Not a company name. A way of being.

I did this exercise years ago. My list had nothing about becoming VP of anything. It had words like: "peace," "impact beyond my company," "time for the people I love," "body that works," "a mind that's still curious."

That list became my compass. Not a GPS with turn-by-turn directions. A compass. North doesn't change even when the terrain does.

Find your person. Study their life. Build your list. That's your 10-year answer.

Who do you admire -- and what does their life tell you about yours?
